## Iran War: The Clock, the Cracks, and the Cost

*Three weeks into a war that nobody officially declared, both sides are negotiating through the same people they're still bombing.*

The 2026 Iran War, now in its fourth week, has reached the phase where both sides simultaneously claim they're winning and quietly signal they want a way out. As of today, Trump has extended his 48-hour ultimatum to strike Iranian power stations — originally issued March 21 — by five days, citing "very good and productive conversations."[^1] Iran denied any direct dialogue took place.[^2] Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf publicly rejected reports of negotiations on X.[^3] ISW notes that Ghalibaf, who apparently *is* conducting back-channel engagement with Washington, has consolidated unusual influence since the war began — which may explain why the public denial and the private talks can coexist.[^3]

The military picture, per ISW, is one of sustained degradation on both sides. The combined US-Israeli force has struck over 3,000 Iranian targets since the war began — including 50+ targets in northern and central Iran overnight March 23–24 — and has destroyed or disabled the majority of Iran's ballistic missile launchers.[^4] Despite this, Iran continues firing missiles at Israel, including one that evaded US and Israeli air defense systems entirely and struck a residential neighborhood in Tel Aviv, damaging apartment buildings and lightly wounding residents.[^5] Cluster munition warheads have been used — a ballistic missile sub-munition impacted in a Haifa suburb.[^4]

Iran has also been deliberately managing its attack scope: ISW observed a decrease in Iranian strikes on Saudi Arabia since March 22, which corroborates Israeli media reports that Tehran decided to limit those attacks to avoid triggering direct Saudi military involvement.[^3] That's a meaningful operational decision — Iran is reading the coalition carefully, probing for the point where a regional partner becomes a direct combatant.

The humanitarian and economic ripples are widening. Philippine President Bongbong Marcos declared a national energy emergency due to fuel supply risks linked to the Strait of Hormuz blockade.[^6] Brent crude briefly rose above $100/barrel before falling 11% on news of US-Iran talks, trading just below that threshold.[^7] Slovenia became the first EU country to institute fuel rationing after drivers from neighboring countries began crossing borders to fill up.[^8] Lebanon expelled Iran's ambassador — declaring him *persona non grata* and giving him until March 29 to leave — citing Iran's financial and military support for Hezbollah.[^9]

On the Israeli front, the military estimates it needs "several more weeks" to complete its war objectives, according to two senior officials who spoke to NPR on condition of anonymity.[^5] "We are in many ways halfway there," one said. Israel Katz, Israel's defense minister, declared the IDF will control a "security zone" up to the Litani River in southern Lebanon until the Hezbollah threat is removed.[^10]

> **KEY:** Trump's claim that the Strait of Hormuz blockade "doesn't really affect" the United States is documented false by FactCheck.org — the US *has* been hit by the global oil price increase, even if a small share of US imports come from the Persian Gulf.[^11] The rhetorical decoupling of the US from the war's economic consequences is the story, not just the inaccuracy. It sets up a domestic frame where the war costs nothing — right as gas prices and energy emergencies spread globally.

## Russo-Ukrainian War: The South Moves, the Front Frays

*Ukraine breaks through in one sector by pulling from another — a strategic gain that creates a structural vulnerability.*

Ukraine's counteroffensive in the south has produced genuine results, but at a price. Ukrainian forces have pushed 10–15 kilometers into the flank and rear of Russian positions in the Dnipropetrovsk-Zaporizhzhia junction area, forcing Moscow to deploy reserves from the Sakhalin-based 68th Army Corps and the 40th Naval Infantry Brigade.[^12] Per Meduza's battlefield analysis, Ukrainian troops have reached Novohryhorivka and the outskirts of Novoivanivka — the latter sits on a key Russian supply route for two combined armies.[^12]

The catch: Ukraine redeployed units that had been defending northern Pokrovsk to execute this counteroffensive. Russian forces have used the gap to advance toward Dobropillia. Meanwhile, Russian troops are closing in on Sloviansk from multiple directions — from Bakhmut, Soledar, and Siversk — with assault units now spotted just 3–4 kilometers from Kalenyky.[^12] The Ukrainian command will need to reinforce that sector, which likely means pulling from somewhere else again.

On infrastructure: a Russian Shahed drone struck and severely damaged the Bernardine Church in Lviv — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — marking a new attack on western Ukraine's cultural landmarks.[^13] Russia also launched 251 drones against Ukraine in a single night.[^14] Ukrainian forces struck oil infrastructure and military assets inside Russia in response.[^14]

US and Ukrainian delegations held another round of bilateral meetings in Miami on March 22.[^14] ISW notes the Middle East war is likely worsening Russia's liquidity problems — wartime spending is already unsustainable, and high energy prices created by the Iran war flow partly to Russia in the short term, but the instability and economic disruption cut both ways.[^14]

Inside Russia this week: a Moscow court found longtime pro-Kremlin blogger Ilya Remeslo — who had just published a tirade calling Putin a "war criminal and a thief" — hospitalized at a St. Petersburg psychiatric facility that historically specialized in punitive psychiatry for Soviet dissidents.[^15] Remeslo had spent years filing complaints against Alexey Navalny for Kremlin money and admitted as much in a detailed interview before being hospitalized. Whether this is punitive psychiatry, voluntary self-preservation from criminal prosecution, or genuine mental distress is unresolved — but the venue choice is doing a lot of work.[^15]

Russia is also actively rewriting history for the next generation: starting September 2026, all schools nationwide switch to a single standardized textbook whose lead author is Putin aide Vladimir Medinsky. The textbook includes Stalin's biography for the first time, reframes Gorbachev as someone who "inherited an empire and left behind its collapse," and requires students to learn the names of war heroes from the "special military operation" to pass the national college exam.[^16]

> **WATCH:** The Meduza report on Ukrainian POW torture in the early days of the invasion — drawn from a Russian military officer who defected three years ago and was sentenced in absentia to seven years for "disinformation" — is worth the full read. What he describes in Zaporizhzhia in 2022 is systematic, named, and specific. He also describes the Russian army's total logistical breakdown in those first weeks. That ground-level account is being buried under larger headlines, but it's part of the permanent record.[^17]

## Afghanistan-Pakistan: The Ceasefire That Isn't

*A temporary ceasefire since March 19 has not stopped the displacement of 115,000 Afghan civilians — this war is still happening.*

The 2026 Afghanistan-Pakistan conflict, which erupted February 21 when Pakistan launched airstrikes in Nangarhar, Paktika, and Khost provinces after a string of terrorist attacks blamed on the Pakistani Taliban and ISIS-K, is under a temporary ceasefire as of March 19.[^18] Pakistan declared "open war" and launched Operation Ghazab Lil Haq — striking targets in Kabul, Kandahar, Paktia, Nangarhar, and other provinces. Both sides dispute casualty figures dramatically. UNAMA confirmed civilian deaths from Pakistani strikes; Pakistan says it killed hundreds of Taliban fighters and destroyed significant infrastructure.[^18]

The humanitarian toll is stark: 115,000 Afghan civilians displaced, 212 Afghan civilians killed per UNAMA, over 20 healthcare facilities in Afghanistan hit by Pakistani strikes.[^18] The Taliban also reportedly forced civilian conscription and restricted domestic media coverage of strikes.

> **WATCH:** This is a state-versus-state conflict — nuclear-armed Pakistan conducting airstrikes on Afghanistan — involving strikes on a capital city (Kabul), claims of corps headquarters destroyed, and over a hundred thousand displaced civilians, all in the span of a month. It is receiving a fraction of the coverage of the Iran war. The ceasefire is described as "temporary" for good reason.

## Other Consequential Events

**Nigerian bombings:** Three coordinated suicide bombings hit Maiduguri, Borno State on March 16, killing 27 people and injuring 146 — striking a teaching hospital, a post office, and a Monday market. No group has claimed responsibility, but Nigerian authorities suspect Boko Haram. The attack ended what authorities described as a "yearslong peaceful stretch" in the city.[^19]

**Danish election:** Danes voted today (March 24) to fill all 179 seats of the Folketing, with Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen seeking a third term. The election follows the Greenland crisis and is a referendum of sorts on Danish sovereignty politics in a rapidly militarizing Europe.[^20]

**Colombia military crash:** At least 66 people were killed when a Colombian Air Force Lockheed C-130 carrying 125 people crashed in Puerto Leguízamo.[^21] One of the worst military aviation disasters in recent Latin American history, with almost no coverage outside the region.

**Africa Cup reversal:** The Confederation of African Football overturned the 2025 Africa Cup of Nations final — originally won by Senegal — and declared Morocco the winner.[^22] Senegal is stripped of the title. No reason widely reported in sources available; the reversal appears to be disciplinary.

**World Baseball Classic:** Venezuela defeated the United States 3–2 in the final at LoanDepot Park in Miami, with Maikel García named tournament MVP.[^23] Venezuela's first WBC championship.

**US abortion access:** A Guttmacher Institute report released today finds the total number of abortions in the United States in 2025 was approximately 1,126,000 — essentially unchanged from 2024, despite extensive state-level bans and restrictions post-Dobbs.[^24] The mechanism keeping the numbers stable: telemedicine and shield laws. 91,000 patients in states with bans received telehealth abortions in 2025; in Louisiana, abortions rose from ~2,500 in 2023 to over 9,000 in 2025, all under a total ban. The legislative response is a federal push to restrict mifepristone by mail, with a federal court ruling expected soon.[^24]

**Kenya floods:** At least 84 people were killed in flash floods across Kenya, including in Kisumu, Uasin Gishu, and Tana River counties.[^25]

**Narva "People's Republic" info op:** Anonymous social media accounts have been promoting a "Narva People's Republic" — calling for secession of Estonia's Ida-Viru County, deliberately echoing the language used for the Donetsk and Luhansk "people's republics" before Russia's 2022 invasion. Estonia's Internal Security Service characterized it as a low-cost information attack; independent investigation found it appears to be a small self-funded operation, not a sophisticated Kremlin project — though who exactly is behind it remains unclear.[^26]

**Robert Mueller, 1944–2026:** The former FBI director and special counsel whose investigation into Russian interference in the 2016 election defined a political era died this week. Wikipedia lists the death; no detailed source in this export.

**Shigeaki Mori, 1937–2026:** Japanese historian and Hiroshima atomic bomb survivor — known internationally for his emotional embrace with President Obama during Obama's 2016 Hiroshima visit — died March 14 at age 88. He spent decades researching Allied POWs who died in the air raids on Japan.[^27]

## Science & Discovery

*NASA pivots from orbit to surface — and a medieval French toilet holds a six-century-old clue to wine continuity.*

**Today's NASA APOD (March 24)** is a video: *A Gravity Map of Earth*, a silent animated map from the GRACE satellite mission showing Earth's gravitational field variations in color and exaggerated topographic relief. A low spot (where you'd feel slightly lighter) lies off the coast of India; a relative high sits in the mountains of Chile. The irregularities trace not just surface features but deep mantle structures. GRACE mapped these variations by tracking tiny changes in the distance between two twin satellites from 2002 to 2017. Watch it at [apod.nasa.gov](https://apod.nasa.gov/apod/astropod.html).[^28]

**Space — NASA pivots to a moon base:** NASA announced it is pausing the Lunar Gateway orbital station project and redirecting $20 billion toward developing a permanent surface base on the Moon, with infrastructure for sustained operations.[^29] This is a significant strategic shift — from a stepping-stone orbital platform to direct surface presence. The orbital station was the architecture of the Biden-era Artemis program; the surface base is a Trump-era redirection toward a more permanent, American-flagged presence on the Moon.

**Biology — Cloning has a hard limit:** Japanese scientists discovered that mammal cloning cannot continue indefinitely. After 20 years and 1,200 mice cloned from a single original, the line failed at generation 58 due to "mutational meltdown" — accumulated genetic errors that overcome any repair mechanism.[^30] The finding constrains assumptions about using cloning for conservation of endangered species and challenges some de-extinction optimism.

**Materials science — Graphene switch:** Researchers from Tel Aviv University achieved highly precise control of graphene's internal structure using a nearly negligible amount of energy — a potential pathway toward ultra-efficient switches for next-generation electronics.[^31]

**Climate — WMO record:** The World Meteorological Organization's annual *State of the Global Climate* report states that Earth's energy imbalance and ocean heat content reached record levels in 2025, driven by rising greenhouse gas emissions.[^32]

**Archaeology / paleobotany:** A 600-year-old grape seed discovered in the toilets of a medieval French hospital is genetically identical to the grapes used to make pinot noir wine today — six centuries of near-perfect agricultural continuity, recovered from a latrine.[^33]

> **KEY:** The NASA pivot away from Lunar Gateway toward a $20B moon base is larger than it's being covered. The Gateway was an international architecture designed around cooperation with ESA, JAXA, and CSA. A surface base, if it's American-built and American-operated, changes who owns the Moon's first permanent infrastructure — and who gets to define the norms of lunar presence. That's a geopolitical question dressed up as a budget decision.[^29]

## Dispatch from the Absurd

The Onion's top story this week: *"ICE Agents Swab Passengers' Hands To Test For Immigrant Residue."*

The joke works because of what it gets exactly right: the logic of contamination, the procedural theater of enforcement-as-performance, the implicit premise that immigrants are a substance rather than people. When The Onion can describe a policy posture this specifically — not a law, not even a proposal, just a *vibe* of the current enforcement moment — it has reached peak saturation. This is the point where the exaggeration and the reality are becoming difficult to distinguish at a glance.[^34]

> **ABSURD:** The Onion's ICE residue gag isn't funny because it's impossible. It's funny because it isn't.[^34]
