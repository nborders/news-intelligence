Russia — The Iran War Is Fixing the Budget; Cementing the Occupation

The oil windfall solves a crisis on paper; ISW documents how Russia is using the window

Russia's federal budget deficit hit 3.45 trillion rubles ($42 billion) by February — 91% of the full-year target in two months, driven by a 47% collapse in oil and gas revenues against a budget built on $59/barrel Urals price.[3] The Iran war changed the math. Urals crude jumped from roughly $45/barrel in February to $75 in March. Each $10 monthly increase generates approximately $1.63 billion in additional tax revenue. A $20 increase sustained for six months adds $38 billion — enough to make the annual budget target achievable.[3]

But today's Meduza analysis is clear that the windfall doesn't fix what's broken. The Central Bank's composite business climate indicator turned negative (-0.1) in March for the first time since October 2022. Investment in fixed capital fell 2.3% in real terms in 2025. Pension replacement rates fell from 31% to 25% between 2018 and 2025. The Finance Ministry has asked agencies to identify 10% of non-military spending for cuts — meaning healthcare, education, and infrastructure bear the cost of a war the oil sector is temporarily funding.[3]

ISW's occupation update today shows how Russia is spending the window. Moscow is planning a road network connecting major occupied Ukrainian cities to incentivize investment and integrate the territories physically into Russia. VTB Bank is expanding mobile banking services into occupied Ukraine. The Nakhimov Naval School is positioning itself as a "hub for training youth in occupied Ukraine on how to develop and operate combat drones."[2] Three Ukrainian teenagers were sentenced by a Russian military court to extensive prison sentences on likely fabricated terrorism charges. A group of Ukrainian children from frontline Zaporizhia Oblast was temporarily deported to Russia's Karelia region — framed as a "propaganda trip."[2]

WatchRussia is explicitly cutting civilian spending — healthcare, education, infrastructure — while using an oil windfall to fund a war economy and using the windfall window to cement permanent occupation infrastructure. The Karelia deportations and drone-training schools are not incidental. They are the long-term strategy.[2][3]

Russia's Information Siege — Synchronized "Delete Telegram" Articles Nationwide

State media traced to a single Far Eastern source; the goal is making the block feel voluntary

A coordinated wave of near-identical articles has appeared across Russian state-controlled regional media — from Kirov to Chelyabinsk to Yoshkar-Ola — all under headlines like "Experts warn: Better delete Telegram before March 31."[4] The articles cite unnamed "lawyers and cybersecurity specialists" and don't explain where the March 31 deadline comes from. Meduza traced all versions to a single February 11 source at PrimPress, a Far Eastern outlet tied to regional authorities. Pro-Kremlin tabloid Baza separately published a piece claiming iPhones are breaking because VPNs overload the processor — technically baseless.[4]

WatchThis is the second layer of the Telegram block. The first layer was throttling the app. The second is propaganda designed to make less tech-savvy Russians believe they want to delete it. The goal is not just restricting access — it's rewriting the story about the restriction.[4]

Science & Discovery

218 gravitational wave chirps — and a 50-year physics prediction confirmed in a Switzerland lab

Black Holes and Neutron Stars: 218 Mergers and Counting — NASA

Each square in today's APOD is a confirmed gravitational wave detection — 218 total from the LIGO-VIRGO-KAGRA Collaboration. The rising frequency plot in each square is a binary pair accelerating toward merger, the final seconds of two objects that may have been orbiting each other for billions of years. Most are black hole–black hole mergers. The universe is violent and continuous. We are cataloguing it one chirp at a time.[1]

Physics — Dark points confirmed faster than light: Researchers at the Technion-Israel Institute of Technology reported in Nature today the first direct experimental measurement of "dark points" — interference features within light wave patterns — confirming a 50-year-old theoretical prediction that these points move superluminally.[6] Nothing physical travels faster than light; the dark point is a geometric feature of the wave, not a particle. But directly measuring them has taken five decades of experimental effort.

Key PointThe LIGO catalogue has 218 entries. In 2015, the first detection was a Nobel Prize. Now it's routine. Science is normalizing the cataloguing of black holes eating each other across the visible universe. That normalization is itself a measurement of how far the field has come.[1]
