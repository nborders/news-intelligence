## The Iran War Crosses a New Threshold
*Energy infrastructure across the entire Gulf is now a battlefield, and the US-Israeli coalition is fracturing in public*

The war entered its most dangerous phase yet this week, as Israel struck Iran's South Pars natural gas field — the largest in the world — and Iran immediately retaliated by hitting Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex (causing "extensive damage," according to QatarEnergy[^1]), oil and gas facilities in the UAE, Kuwait, and Saudi Arabia.[^2] Qatar has halted all LNG production. Japan is releasing 80 million barrels from its strategic reserves — the largest release in its history.[^3] The US is seeking an additional $200 billion from Congress to fund the war.[^4]

The decapitation campaign intensified dramatically. Israeli forces assassinated Iranian Intelligence Minister Esmaeil Khatib — the man responsible for coordinating internal repression — in an overnight airstrike in Tehran.[^5] Earlier in the week, both Supreme National Security Council secretary Ali Larijani and Basij commander Gholamreza Soleimani were killed in separate strikes.[^6] ISW notes that the combined decapitation campaign is reportedly causing paranoia among Iranian regime officials and security services.[^7]

The US deployed GBU-72 bunker-buster bombs in combat for the first time, destroying underground Iranian anti-ship cruise missile sites.[^8] But on the ground-level air war, Iran is winning on cost: Shaheds cost $20,000–50,000 each; US interceptors cost millions. NPR reports that US officials, speaking off the record, are already concerned about running out of interceptor missiles before Iran runs out of drones.[^9] Ukraine offered its low-cost "Sting" interceptor program to the US. Trump declined on Fox News: "We don't need their help."[^10]

Meanwhile, multiple NATO allies — Germany, Italy, Spain, Australia — have explicitly ruled out sending warships to help lift the Strait of Hormuz blockade.[^11] The Wall Street Journal reports Russia is actively sharing satellite intelligence on US base locations with Iran.[^12]

FactCheck flags two important claims in circulation: Trump's assertion that the Hormuz blockade "doesn't really affect" the US is rated misleading — while US oil imports from the Gulf are modest, global oil price increases do affect the US economy.[^13] FactCheck also documents Trump's claim that Iran posed "a nuclear threat" as disputed by arms control experts who say there's "a lack of evidence" that Iran had rebuilt enrichment facilities.[^14]

> **KEY:** The US and Israel are no longer fighting the same war. Trump publicly announced the US "knew nothing about" Israel's South Pars strike and warned Israel not to do it again — while simultaneously threatening Iran that the US would "massively blow up the entire South Pars Gas Field" if Iranian attacks on Qatar continue.[^15] The coalition's coordination is visibly breaking down in public, and that incoherence is itself a signal Iran will read.[^7]

## The Kabul Hospital Airstrike
*Pakistan bombed a drug rehab center in Afghanistan, killing over 140 confirmed dead — and almost nobody is covering it*

On March 16, Pakistani jets destroyed the Omid Addiction Treatment Hospital in Kabul, a drug rehabilitation center treating approximately 3,000 patients.[^16] Afghanistan claims 408 dead and 265 wounded. AFP and BBC journalists confirmed at least 30 dead in initial reporting. UNAMA, the UN mission to Afghanistan, confirmed at least 143 killed and 119 wounded and is continuing its investigation.[^17]

Pakistan denies targeting the facility, claiming it struck military installations nearby. The Afghan government, UNAMA, and international NGOs all say the hospital was directly hit. Large sections of the compound — including a prayer hall and five residential blocks — were completely destroyed.[^16]

This is a massive civilian casualty event — larger than most events that dominate news cycles — happening in a country that has been essentially invisible in Western coverage since the US withdrawal. The Afghanistan-Pakistan conflict is an escalating bilateral war with its own logic: Pakistani airstrikes, Afghan mortar fire into Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, cross-border retaliations. This week, Afghan forces also shelled a residential building in Bajaur District, killing four members of the same family.[^18]

> **WATCH:** The Kabul hospital strike has received a fraction of a percent of the coverage given to Iranian attacks on Gulf oil infrastructure. By any human calculus, 143+ confirmed civilians killed in a hospital bombing is the week's most catastrophic single event. That it's happening in Afghanistan — and that Pakistan is the perpetrator — seems to be why it disappears.[^16]

## Russia's Information Siege: Telegram, Spies, and Sick Cows
*The Kremlin moved two weeks early to block its most-used independent messenger — and one of its elite hit-squad agents got caught because of Google Translate*

**The Telegram block:** Russia expected to block Telegram on April 1. They appear to have started March 14–15, two weeks early.[^19] As of this week, one in four messages fails to reach recipients; on some networks, 80% of Telegram traffic is being intercepted.[^20] The reaction inside Russia has been sharp and public — Meduza collected social media comments ranging from furious to sardonic: *"Wake up already,"* *"You're sawing off the branch you're sitting on."*[^20]

The cruelest irony, documented by Meduza: the Kremlin built Max, its own state-controlled messenger, as a Telegram replacement. But the Kremlin itself reportedly told soldiers at the front that Max is too insecure to use for military coordination — so troops will apparently keep Telegram access while civilians lose it.[^19] The editor-in-chief of one of Russia's largest independent Telegram news channels told Meduza: "They don't have a choice. Max it is, then."[^21]

**Center 795:** The Insider reported this week on Center 795, a top-secret Russian hit squad established in December 2022, embedded inside the Kalashnikov arms manufacturer for cover, reporting directly to General Staff Chief Gerasimov. Around 500 officers, handpicked from FSB, GRU, Rosgvardia, and the Kremlin protection force, with department heads earning ~$7,800/month and the commander pulling ~$500,000/year.[^22]

One of its agents, Denis Alimov, was arrested at Bogotá Airport on February 24 while allegedly orchestrating assassination attempts against Chechen dissidents in Europe. He had been coordinating with his hitman via Google Translate — copying encrypted app messages into Google's servers — which gave the FBI a real-time surveillance window under a warrant.[^22] "The most secretive unit in Russian military intelligence was undone not by a defector or a mole — but by a language barrier and a warrant."

**Mysterious livestock culls:** A domestic story getting almost no Western coverage: for two weeks, Russian authorities in Novosibirsk Oblast have been confiscating and destroying thousands of livestock animals — cows, bulls, pigs, sheep, camels — citing an unspecified "especially dangerous disease" while refusing to name it.[^23] Farmers, given no documentation or justification, have been blocking the cull vehicles. The circumstantial evidence (all cloven-hoofed animals within 5km radius culled, Kazakhstan and Belarus banning Siberian livestock imports, a governor apparently acknowledging foot-and-mouth at a closed meeting) points to a foot-and-mouth outbreak being concealed to protect export trade. Kazakhstan and Belarus have already responded with import bans.[^23]

**Ukraine:** Meduza's latest battlefield analysis shows Ukraine executing a tactical counteroffensive in the Zaporizhzhia region — pushing 10–15km into Russian rear positions near Novohryhorivka — but the tradeoff is that units were redeployed from Pokrovsk's northern defenses, allowing Russia to open a new offensive toward Dobropillia.[^24] ISW confirms Russian advances near Slovyansk and Pokrovsk, and Russia launched 147 drones at Ukraine on March 18 alone.[^7] Ukraine sent 201 military experts to Middle Eastern countries to assist with Shahed drone defense — the same expertise Trump declined to accept for US forces.

> **KEY:** The Telegram block is not primarily about security — it's about replacing an open information ecosystem with a closed, state-controlled one before the war's political cost becomes too visible domestically. The Kremlin's own press secretary admitted: "We don't operate in these [open] environments — so we need to figure out what we will do going forward."[^19] That's not a security rationale. That's a media strategy admission.

## Sudanese Civil War Crosses into Chad
*The RSF's drone war is now killing people at funerals across an international border*

An RSF drone strike killed at least 16 people at a funeral in the border town of Tiné, Chad on March 18.[^25] A day later, Chadian government officials confirmed a second drone strike, raising the death toll to 17.[^26] Chadian President Mahamat Déby ordered his military to retaliate against both the RSF and the Sudanese Armed Forces.[^25]

The Sudanese civil war has been progressively externalizing — Iranian drones supplied to government forces, mass civilian casualties, now cross-border strikes killing mourners at funerals in a neighboring country. Sudan's war has killed more people than Ukraine and Gaza combined in the past two years and receives a fraction of the coverage.

Its withdrawal — framed as procedural — is diplomatically significant.

## Science & Discovery
*A week defined by deep-time discoveries and a drone war getting unexpected scientific attention*

![NASA APOD — Launch Plume: SpaceX Jellyfish](https://apod.nasa.gov/apod/image/2603/jellyfish.jpg)
*Launch Plume: SpaceX Jellyfish* — Michael Seeley

**Space:** The Mars buried delta story is significant. NASA's Perseverance rover found a river delta more than 35 meters beneath the surface of Jezero Crater using ground-penetrating radar.[^37] This is the deepest evidence yet of ancient water activity at the mission site and changes the stratigraphic picture for where biosignatures might be preserved. Meanwhile, astronomers published a list of 45 rocky, potentially habitable exoplanets — the most targeted search candidate list to date — out of 6,000+ known exoplanets.[^38] ESA's JUICE probe is also being prepared to study Jupiter's "minor" 94 moons alongside its primary Ganymede/Europa targets.[^39]

**Geology:** Washington State University researchers published findings explaining how Idaho's Silver Valley and Cobalt Belt mineral deposits formed more than 1.2 billion years ago from ancient brines.[^40] The Belt Supergroup — stretching across eastern Washington, Idaho, and Montana — is the most significant domestic cobalt source, a strategic mineral for batteries. Understanding deposit formation could reframe how the US approaches domestic critical minerals extraction.

**Climate/Engineering:** A Phys.org study directly relevant to the Iran war: a Supply Chain Intelligence Institute Austria analysis confirms that a prolonged Strait of Hormuz closure would severely disrupt global supply chains far beyond oil prices — affecting fertilizer, plastics feedstock, and industrial inputs.[^41] That's not economic modeling; that's a map of second-order consequences already unfolding.

> **KEY:** The Perseverance radar discovery of the buried delta changes the excavation calculus for Mars sample return. Biosignatures, if they exist, are more likely to be preserved at depth — which the mission's current surface-level sample collection was not designed to reach.[^37]

## Dispatch from the Absurd

*One Battle After Another* won Best Picture at the 98th Academy Awards on March 15 — six Oscars total, including the newly created Best Casting award.[^42] The film also beat *Sinners*, which received a record-breaking 16 nominations (the most in Oscar history, surpassing *All About Eve* and *Titanic*) but won only four.[^43]

The title *One Battle After Another*, applied to a Best Picture winner in the third week of an active war in Iran, achieved a level of unintentional commentary that no satirist could improve on. Conan O'Brien hosted.

> **ABSURD:** The Onion's contribution this week: "Strait of Hormones." A pun so bad it's perfect — the Strait of Hormuz, which has become the most consequential geographic choke point since the Suez Crisis, translated into a pubescent metaphor for a conflict nobody understands and everybody is sweating about. Saturation confirmed.[^44]
