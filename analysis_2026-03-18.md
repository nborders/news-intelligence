## Iran War: Decapitation Campaign and the Hormuz Trap
*Three senior officials killed in 24 hours — and a strategic dilemma nobody is saying out loud*

Wikipedia's Current Events portal tracks the Iran war as the week's dominant ongoing event, with three of Iran's most senior officials killed by Israel in approximately 24 hours.[^1] Intelligence Minister Esmaeil Khatib was assassinated in a Tehran airstrike overnight March 17–18.[^2] The previous day, Supreme National Security Council Secretary Ali Larijani — one of the most influential figures in Iranian foreign and domestic policy across four decades — and Basij paramilitary commander Gholamreza Soleimani were killed in separate strikes.[^3] Iran responded with a Khorramshahr missile strike on Ramat Gan in central Israel, killing an elderly couple who could not reach a shelter in time.[^4]

NPR's Iran expert Karim Sadjadpour offers the sharpest framing of where this is going: what began as a "war of choice" — with no imminent nuclear or missile threat to the US — has become a "war of necessity" because Iran immediately regionalized the conflict, closed the Strait of Hormuz, and drew in the Gulf states. Trump has acknowledged he was surprised by Iran's response.[^5]

> **KEY:** ISW's strategic trap: the US and Israel are trying to *demonstrate* they can deny Iran the ability to disrupt Hormuz. If they stop the war before that demonstration is complete, Iran learns the extortion threshold is low — and a weakened surviving regime can disrupt shipping at will in future crises with even less fear of consequence.[^6]

The coalition picture is fracturing. Germany, Italy, Spain, and Australia have all ruled out sending warships to help lift the Hormuz blockade, despite a formal US request.[^7] Pakistan negotiated safe passage for one oil tanker by agreeing to pay in Chinese yuan rather than dollars — a small but symbolically significant crack in dollar-denominated oil trade.[^8] Trump waived the Jones Act for 60 days to try to lower US gas prices, now at $3.84/gallon, up roughly 80 cents. Experts say the waiver will have minimal effect.[^9]

FactCheck has been doing important work here. Trump claimed the Hormuz blockade "doesn't really affect" the US the way it does other countries — technically true about direct oil imports, but false about global price impact: the US has been hit by the worldwide crude price surge.[^10] On the legal question: multiple Democrats have claimed Trump didn't have authority to unilaterally order the Feb. 28 strikes that killed Ayatollah Khamenei.[^11] And FactCheck documented that Trump attributed the bombing of an Iranian girls' school to Iran; video, satellite images, and expert analysis indicate the US was likely responsible.[^12]

One notable US domestic development that branches off the war: National Counterterrorism Center director Joe Kent resigned in protest, stating Iran posed no imminent threat to the United States.[^13]

## Russia-Ukraine War: Telegram Blocked, Front Lines Hold
*Russia kills its own communication infrastructure while sharing military intel with Iran*

Wikipedia tracks the Russo-Ukrainian war as ongoing, with EU sanctions this week against nine Russians for their roles in the Bucha massacre.[^14] The front lines, per ISW, are operationally stable — Ukraine liberated more territory than Russia seized in February, despite Gerasimov's continued public claims of imminent Ukrainian collapse.[^15]

The most consequential off-battlefield development is Russia's early block of Telegram. The block was expected in April; it began the weekend of March 14–15, two weeks ahead of schedule, affecting mobile and desktop versions across the country.[^16] Meduza reports growing anger among ordinary Russians who relied on Telegram for everything from news to business communication — not just dissidents.[^17] ISW notes the Russian MoD has simultaneously issued orders prohibiting soldiers from using Telegram, suggesting the military saw the app as an OPSEC liability.[^18]

> **WATCH:** ISW confirmed this week that Russia is actively sharing satellite imagery of US military base locations with Iran, along with improved drone technology.[^21] The Russia-Iran axis has moved from political alignment to documented operational military partnership.

Two Meduza investigations are worth flagging. First: Kazakhstan, previously a refuge for anti-war Russians who fled after 2022, is reportedly preparing a mass deportation campaign coordinated with Moscow — narrowing what little safe haven remained for Russian exiles.[^19] Second: Russia's elite assassination unit "Center 795," established in December 2022, was exposed because one of its agents used Google Translate to communicate covertly, leaving a traceable digital signature.[^20]

## Afghanistan-Pakistan: Disputed Airstrike, 400 Claimed Dead
*A nuclear-armed state bombing a neighbor — getting almost no coverage*

Wikipedia flags the 2026 Afghanistan-Pakistan conflict as a distinct tracked event, and the numbers this week are staggering in their dispute. Afghanistan claims Pakistani jets bombed a drug rehabilitation center in Kabul, killing over 400 people. Pakistan says it struck military installations only. AFP and BBC journalists on the ground confirmed at least 30 dead — far fewer than Afghanistan's claim, but enough to confirm civilian casualties.[^22]

> **WATCH:** This conflict is receiving almost no coverage relative to Iran. A nuclear-armed state (Pakistan) is conducting airstrikes on a neighbor while Afghan forces fire mortars back across the border — four members of one family killed this week in Pakistan's Bajaur District.[^23] This has escalation written on it.

## Nigeria: Bombings and Bandits
*Two separate mass-casualty events in one week, mentioned nowhere outside Wikipedia*

At least 23 people were killed and 108 injured in suspected suicide bombings at three sites in Maiduguri — including the Monday Market and the University of Maiduguri Teaching Hospital — with no group claiming responsibility.[^24] A separate attack by armed bandits in Jibia, Katsina State, killed at least 18 people.[^25]

Both appear in Wikipedia's Current Events as standalone entries, not buried as sub-bullets under a larger story. That editorial choice reflects something about significance that the news cycle is not.

## US Domestic: Institutions Under Pressure
*Courts and agencies in standoff with the executive branch on multiple fronts*

A federal judge blocked RFK Jr.'s revisions to the childhood vaccination schedule, ruling the changes likely violated required procedures.[^26] FactCheck found the FDA Commissioner had publicly implied — falsely — that a leucovorin approval covered hundreds of thousands of autistic children when it covered only a rare genetic subgroup.[^27]

On Medicaid: NPR reports that Minnesota's Medicaid funds — hundreds of millions, possibly billions of dollars — are in limbo as the Trump administration pursues a fraud investigation. The case may set precedent for how the administration can unilaterally freeze funds to states.[^28]

> **KEY:** A federal court ordered all 1,042 Voice of America employees put on leave by Kari Lake back to work, ruling her actions "arbitrary and capricious." A prior court finding had already concluded Lake was likely ineligible for the executive role she assumed at the agency.[^29] The pattern: courts repeatedly blocking executive branch actions on procedural grounds — which tends to be slow and legally sound but exhausting to maintain.

## Other Consequential Events
*Things that deserve more attention than they're getting*

**Belgium/Lumumba:** A Brussels court ordered former Belgian diplomat Étienne Davignon to stand trial for participation in war crimes in connection with the 1961 assassination of Congolese Prime Minister Patrice Lumumba. Davignon is the only surviving accused.[^30] This is the first time a Western diplomat has been ordered to stand trial for a Cold War-era political assassination conducted with European complicity.

**Argentina exits WHO:** Argentina's foreign minister formally announced withdrawal from the World Health Organization.[^31] During a week when a meningitis outbreak at a UK university also appeared in Wikipedia's Current Events, the timing is worth noting.

**Cuba:** Authorities arrested five people for damaging a Communist Party office during protests over prolonged blackouts and food shortages.[^32] Separately, Cuba announced citizens abroad can now own domestic businesses — economic liberalization while political crackdowns continue.[^33]

**Ecuador:** 75,000 soldiers and police deployed to enforce an 11pm–5am curfew in four provinces. Colombian President Petro accused Ecuador's military of conducting an airstrike inside Colombia that killed 27 people.[^34]
