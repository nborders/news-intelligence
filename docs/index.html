IRGC Navy Commander killed; US claims 92% of naval assets destroyed

The IDF announced today that it killed IRGC Navy Commander Alireza Tangsiri and his Intelligence Deputy Bahnam Rezaei in a strike on Bandar Abbas, Hormozgan Province.[1] Tangsiri was the officer who oversaw Iran's campaign to disrupt maritime traffic and threaten freedom of navigation through the Strait of Hormuz — the man operationally responsible for the toll road that Iran has built on the world's most important oil chokepoint.[2] This follows the earlier killing of the IRGC 1st Naval District commander in Bandar Abbas, also this week.

Despite the decapitation of Iran's naval command, the toll road keeps operating. That gap — between destroying the individuals who built a strategy and destroying the strategy itself — is the defining analytical story of this war's fourth week. US CENTCOM Commander Brad Cooper reported today that the combined force has now struck over 10,000 Iranian military targets, destroyed 92% of Iran's naval fleet's "largest vessels," and eliminated over two-thirds of Iran's missile, drone, and naval production facilities.[2] Iran's missile and drone launch rates are down approximately 90% from the war's beginning.[2] Yet Iran continues to operate the Hormuz toll system, continues to fire missiles into Israel, and continues to refuse the US peace framework.

The diplomatic track acquired texture today. Middle East special envoy Steve Witkoff confirmed publicly that the United States did in fact share a 15-point peace plan with Iran through Pakistan — ending days of ambiguity about whether the plan existed at all.[1] Pakistani Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar confirmed that indirect negotiations between the US and Iran are taking place.[1] Both confirmations matter: they establish that there is a real diplomatic channel, even as Iran fires seven salvos of ballistic missiles at Israel and both sides deny the war is over.

The regional blast radius continues to expand. Two people were killed in Abu Dhabi today by falling debris from an intercepted Iranian ballistic missile — a reminder that Iran's targeting extends beyond Israel.[1] Uganda's Chief of Defence Forces Muhoozi Kainerugaba said today that Uganda is "ready to join the war on the side of Israel."[1] Iran's Ministry of Sport banned the Iranian national football team from traveling to "hostile countries," effectively raising serious doubt about Iran's participation in this year's FIFA World Cup — being held in the United States, Canada, and Mexico.[1] Norway's parliament cut petrol and diesel taxes in response to surging energy prices. Mauritius and South Sudan announced electricity rationing.[1] ISW notes the conflict has "shifted from a sharp inflection point to a more complex challenge" — approaching one month of active war.[2]

Key PointThree IRGC naval commanders are now dead. Iran's largest naval vessels are 92% gone. Iran's missile production is being systematically destroyed. And Iran is still winning the war it's fighting — the asymmetric one, where the Hormuz toll persists, missiles keep landing in Tel Aviv, and every passing week locks the oil price floor higher. The US can destroy Iran's military capacity faster than Iran can rebuild it. What the US cannot destroy is the institutional logic that keeps the toll road running without Tangsiri.[2]

Russia — The Iran War Is Solving the Kremlin's Budget Problem

Moscow's January–February deficit was 91% of the full-year target; the Iran war changed the math — but not the structural problems

Russia entered 2026 in fiscal crisis. By the end of February, the federal budget deficit had reached 3.45 trillion rubles ($42 billion) — 91% of the full-year target, reached in two months.[3] Oil and gas revenues had collapsed 47% year-on-year, against a budget built around an optimistic $59/barrel Urals price. The National Wealth Fund was being drawn down at nearly $5 billion per month.[3]

Then Trump launched the Iran war, and Tehran blocked the Strait of Hormuz. Russia is one of the few major oil exporters whose exports don't route through the strait. Urals crude jumped from roughly $45/barrel in February to roughly $75 in March.[3] Every $10 increase in monthly Urals price generates approximately $1.63 billion in additional Russian tax revenue. A sustained $20 increase for six months would add $38 billion.[3] The budget target that looked impossible in February now looks achievable — Russia's annual oil and gas revenue figure "looks achievable, with only a modest shortfall at most," according to a leading Russian economist.[3]

This is the part that is not being widely discussed: Russia has a direct financial interest in the Iran war continuing at a moderate intensity. Not a full-scale global recession that crushes oil demand. Not a negotiated peace that reopens Hormuz. A moderately prolonged conflict that keeps prices elevated.[3] Moscow and Tehran are not coordinating. They don't need to. Their incentives are structurally aligned.

Meanwhile, ISW's occupation update today shows Russia is using the war period to deepen the permanent annexation of occupied Ukrainian territory: planning a road network connecting major occupied cities to incentivize investment and integrate the territories into Russia's infrastructure; expanding VTB Bank's mobile banking presence in occupied Ukraine; and — most striking — the Russian Nakhimov Naval School is positioning itself as a "central hub for training youth in occupied Ukraine on how to develop and operate combat drones."[2] Three Ukrainian teenagers were sentenced by a Russian military court to "extensive prison sentences on likely fabricated terrorism charges."[2] Russian authorities temporarily deported a group of Ukrainian children from frontline areas of occupied Zaporizhia Oblast to Russia's Republic of Karelia — framed as a "propaganda trip."[2]

The oil windfall won't fix what's broken. Russia's Central Bank composite business climate indicator turned negative (-0.1) in March for the first time since October 2022.[3] Investment in fixed capital fell 2.3% in real terms in 2025. The pension replacement rate fell from 31% to 25% between 2018 and 2025 as war deaths and emigration shrink the working population. Military spending consumes 16.8 trillion rubles this year ($204 billion); civilian economy spending is being cut 10%.[3]

WatchRussia is explicitly cutting healthcare, education, and infrastructure — while using occupied Ukrainian youth to train the next generation of drone operators — while an oil windfall covers the gaps temporarily. This is what a war economy looks like two years in: the military sector cannibalizes the civilian one, and a foreign war provides just enough revenue to keep it running.[3][2]

Russia's Information Siege — Synchronized "Delete Telegram" Articles Appear Nationwide

State-controlled regional media are running near-identical articles with a single message: delete the app before March 31

In the past two weeks, a coordinated wave of near-identical articles has appeared across Russian state-controlled regional media outlets — from Kirov to Chelyabinsk to Yoshkar-Ola — all carrying essentially the same headline: "Experts warn: Better delete Telegram before March 31."[4] The articles cite unnamed "lawyers and cybersecurity specialists" and warn of risks ranging from phishing to data theft. None name actual experts. None explain where the March 31 deadline comes from.

Meduza traced the articles to a single February source at PrimPress, a Far Eastern outlet tied to regional authorities, and identified the coordinated republication as deliberate infrastructure propaganda.[4] The pro-Kremlin tabloid Baza — now owned by Putin friend Yuri Kovalchuk's media empire — separately published a piece claiming that Russians' iPhones are overheating and breaking because VPNs used to access Telegram "overload the processor."[4] This claim has no technical basis.

The pattern: Russia throttled Telegram earlier than announced in early March, blocking it faster than expected. Now, rather than simply blocking the app, state media is conducting a parallel campaign to make the block feel voluntary — to persuade less tech-savvy users that they should delete the app for their own safety. The Kremlin isn't just shutting down Russia's most significant alternative information channel. It's trying to rewrite why.

WatchThe Telegram block removed the last mass-reach independent news channel inside Russia. The synchronized propaganda campaign attempting to frame that block as a user safety measure represents a maturation of information control — not just restricting access but managing the story about the restriction. The goal isn't just that Russians can't read Meduza. It's that they believe they shouldn't want to.[4]

Afghanistan–Pakistan — Hot War, No Framework

Shelling resumed after the five-day ceasefire collapsed; Pakistan is mediating the Iran war while fighting its own

The ceasefire between Afghanistan and Pakistan — which ran March 19 to March 24 — collapsed last night with resumed artillery exchanges in Kunar Province.[1] The conflict, now in its second month (started February 21), involves Pakistan's military and the Taliban government of Afghanistan, with the Pakistani Taliban (TTP) as an overlapping belligerent. UNHCR estimates 115,000 Afghan civilians have been displaced.[5]

Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif is simultaneously offering to host Iran–US peace talks and shelling his eastern neighbor. There is no visible international diplomatic framework managing escalation risk in this conflict. Both states have nuclear weapons.

Sudan — 16 More Civilians Killed in El Fasher

RSF attacks continue in North Darfur while the world's attention remains elsewhere

The Sudan Doctors Network reported that Rapid Support Forces killed 16 civilians, including three women, in El Fasher, North Darfur, on March 25.[1] The Al Deain Teaching Hospital drone strike death toll remains at 70. The RSF, alongside SPLM-N rebels, holds Kurmuk in the Blue Nile region near the Ethiopian border, extending rebel control eastward.[1]

The Sudanese civil war is now in its third year and has produced one of the world's worst humanitarian crises. It receives roughly 1% of the coverage of the Iran war.

Science & Discovery

218 gravitational wave detections, 45 exoplanet candidates for life, and antimatter on a Swiss road

Black Holes and Neutron Stars: 218 Mergers and Counting — NASA

Each square in today's APOD represents a gravitational wave detection — 218 total confirmed by the LIGO-VIRGO-KAGRA Collaboration. The rising frequency "chirp" in each plot shows a binary pair accelerating toward merger. Most detections are black hole–black hole mergers, because black holes are heavier, louder, and visible farther away. These events are continuous throughout the cosmos. We are cataloguing the universe's most violent processes one chirp at a time.

Space — 45 worlds worth watching: On March 25, astronomers published a shortlist of 45 nearby exoplanets as the most promising candidates for habitability — selected based on size, composition, and location within their stars' habitable zones. The catalogue is designed to guide observations by next-generation telescopes in the search for biosignatures and extraterrestrial life.[6] At just 10.7 light-years, GJ 887 d — a super-Earth discovered March 6 — is the second-nearest known exoplanet in a habitable zone.[6]

Engineering — Antimatter takes a road trip: On March 24, scientists in Switzerland transported antimatter by road for the first time — an estimated 100 to 1,000 antiprotons in a Penning trap, carried 5 kilometers to assess stability during transit.[6] This is a proof-of-concept for portable antimatter: the ability to move it opens new experimental configurations. Antimatter annihilates on contact with regular matter; carrying it through traffic is as absurd and careful as it sounds.

Medicine — Lab-grown oesophagus works: Scientists at Great Ormond Street Hospital and University College London reported on March 20 the first successful implantation of a lab-grown oesophagus in a growing animal — restoring full swallowing function without immunosuppression.[6] The technique offers potential treatment for children born with missing or damaged esophageal tissue, one of the more difficult congenital conditions to manage surgically.

Climate — The warming rate doubled: A study published March 9 in Geophysical Research Letters confirmed with statistical confidence that global warming has accelerated since around 2015 — the rate of increase roughly doubled from 0.2°C to 0.35°C per decade after accounting for natural variability. If current trends continue, the 1.5°C threshold could be reached before 2030.[6]

Pacific Northwest — Snow drought with few parallels: The 2026 water year has been marked by an extraordinary snow drought across the Pacific Northwest and wider western US, with The Conversation describing it as having "few parallels in recent history."[7] For a region that depends on snowpack for summer water supply, wildfire defense, and agricultural irrigation, an off-year is a stress test. This one is not ordinary.

Key PointThe 45-exoplanet shortlist is astronomy telling us exactly which nearby worlds deserve the most scrutiny for signs of life. Astronomers are doing this methodically and carefully, ranking candidates, designing instruments, planning decades of observation. Meanwhile, on the one confirmed inhabited planet in the dataset, today's news includes: naval commanders assassinated, teenagers imprisoned on fabricated charges, hospitals bombed in Sudan, and antimatter being driven around Switzerland in a thermos. We are looking for the right things in the right places. Whether we deserve to find them is a separate question.[6]
