Iran rejects 15-point plan; Hormuz becomes a toll road

The afternoon brought the clearest diplomatic signal yet from Tehran: Iran formally rejected the US 15-point peace proposal, setting five conditions of its own for a ceasefire.[1] A senior Iranian official told Reuters the country was "still reviewing" despite the initial negative response — diplomatic language that could mean anything from "we're negotiating in private" to "we're stalling for time."[1] Iran's parliament speaker called the talk of negotiations "fake news intended to manipulate financial and oil markets."[2] The Foreign Ministry simultaneously acknowledged responding "through intermediaries of friendly countries."[2]

The military tempo did not pause during the diplomacy. ISW's afternoon update reports Iran has launched six additional barrages of missiles targeting Israel since this morning's cutoff, with one hitting near a power station in Hadera, northern Israel, and another impacting an industrial zone in the Negev.[3] Cluster munitions continued — ISW describes Iran firing them "likely to maximize destruction and terrorize Israeli civilians."[3] A cluster bomb wounded nine people in Bnei Brak, including six children and an elderly woman.[1] Jordan's air force intercepted four Iranian missiles and a drone.[1] A drone struck a fuel tank at Kuwait International Airport, causing a fire.[1] ISW also confirmed that the IDF struck a facility in Esfahan Province responsible for designing and developing submarines and support systems — expanding the target set beyond just missile infrastructure.[3]

The strangest new development remains the Hormuz toll system. Iran has required over 20 vessels to transit via a "Tehran-approved route" through Iranian territorial waters, with some reportedly paying a fee.[3] Ships associated with the US, Israel, or other "participants in the aggression" are explicitly excluded from safe passage.[3] This is not a closed strait — it's a selective one, operated by a state at war, with an informal entrance fee. COSCO Shipping resumed container bookings to Gulf countries after Iran acknowledged non-hostile vessels could pass.[1] Gold prices in Dubai, which had dropped for 15 consecutive days on diplomacy signals, rose again after Iran's rejection.[1]

Iran also claimed it has intelligence that the US and an "unnamed regional country" are planning to invade and capture Kharg Island in the Persian Gulf — as thousands of US Marines are scheduled to arrive in the region in the coming days.[1] The claim serves Iran's domestic narrative regardless of its accuracy. Pakistan has offered to host US-Iran talks, with Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif publicly tagging Trump and Iran's foreign minister on social media and saying his country "stands ready" to facilitate.[2] The strategic triangle of Pakistan-as-mediator, US-as-attacker, and Iran-as-party is notable given that Pakistan is simultaneously fighting its own border war with Afghanistan.

Key PointIran's five-condition counteroffer to the 15-point US plan is more strategically significant than a flat rejection. It signals Tehran wants a negotiated exit — one that preserves something. The conditions themselves would be the story if Iran releases them publicly. What Iran asks for in exchange for ending this war will tell you what Iran thinks it's already won.[1][3]
WatchIsrael's defense minister declared the IDF will control a security zone up to the Litani River in southern Lebanon until "the threat of Hezbollah is removed" — and suggested Israel would remain there after the conflict ends.[2] Lebanon's death toll from Israeli strikes has reached 1,039 since March 2, with more than 1 million displaced.[2] Lebanon expelled Iran's ambassador (deadline: March 29). A Lebanon permanent occupation is being built in plain sight while everyone watches the Iran negotiations.

Russo-Ukrainian War — The Largest Strike, and a Missile Launcher Destroyed

Russia launched 1,000 drones and missiles in one night; Ukraine destroyed a Zirkon launcher in Crimea

Russia launched nearly 1,000 drones and missiles against Ukraine over the nights of March 23-24 — the single largest strike series in the entire war to date.[4] ISW describes this as "a significant inflection in Russian strike tactics" — not just more of the same, but a qualitative shift toward saturation targeting that can overwhelm air defenses by volume alone, "threaten more areas of Ukraine for longer periods of time," and disproportionately hit civilian areas.[4] Russian forces killed at least three people across the barrage, damaging infrastructure in Poltava, Zaporizhzhia, and multiple other oblasts.[5]

One strike destroyed the Bernardine Church in Lviv's Old Town, a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The church survived centuries; it did not survive the night of March 23.[4]

Ukraine delivered a notable counter: Ukrainian forces destroyed a ground-based Zirkon hypersonic missile launcher while it was moving into a firing position in Crimea on the night of March 23-24.[4] ISW flagged this as "a difficult task" — detecting and hitting a mobile Zirkon launcher before it fires requires intelligence access, precision targeting, and timing. Separately, a Ukrainian overnight strike halted operations at a Russian oil export port.[4]

On the ground, the Meduza battlefield map (as of March 19) shows Ukraine's Zaporizhzhia counteroffensive remains active — pushing 10-15 kilometers into the rear of Russian positions near the 5th and 36th Combined Arms Armies — but the trade-off is real: Ukrainian units redeployed south from north of Pokrovsk, allowing Russia to launch a new push toward Dobropillia.[6] The approach toward Sloviansk from Bakhmut, Soledar, and Siversk also continues, with Russian assault units within 3-4 kilometers of Kalenyky.

This morning's ISW Korean Peninsula update adds an underreported dimension: North Korea reinforced its "two hostile states" doctrine against South Korea at the March 22 SPA session and is integrating lessons from the Ukraine war to improve its military equipment and tactics.[7] ISW assessed a potential meeting between Trump and Kim Jong Un during Trump's upcoming PRC visit as "unlikely," noting the US has not changed its North Korea policy to meet Kim's requirements for dialogue.[7]

Key PointThe 1,000-drone strike is not just larger — it's structurally different. Russia is running a saturation model: flood the airspace, exhaust interceptor supplies, extend the threat clock. Ukraine's air defenses were built for a smaller problem. The new operational envelope demands new solutions.[4]

Russia's Information Siege — Three Stories, One Picture

Scarlet Swan, FSB sabotage ads, and school textbooks you have to learn to graduate

Three Meduza stories today form a coherent map of Russia's domestic information management as year five of the war continues.

Scarlet Swan is a protest movement calling Russians to take to the streets on March 29 against internet restrictions — and it may be a trap.[8] The movement appeared simultaneously with a call for protests by exiled political consultant Dmitry Kisiev, acquired a distinctive red-paper-swan identity, collected personal data from more than 40 volunteers (including teenagers as young as 15 and 17), and then began falling apart in public: accounts sold, administrators threatening each other, messages calling for violence later deleted. Security journalists have noted the pattern resembles previous FSB operations that built quasi-organizations, then used the structure to classify them as extremist. The movement's Telegram channel had 8,800 subscribers as of this morning. Kisiev himself denies any connection but said he doesn't believe it's FSB-linked. A Kremlin-adjacent Telegram channel called it a "simulacrum project" designed to contain protest energy. Police already searched the home of one organizer (who then turned himself in to prosecutors).[8]

The FSB sabotage experiment is a different kind of operation. The Sverdlovsk FSB branch ran Telegram ads offering "easy money" — couriering and sabotage, with up to $37,000 for taking out infrastructure — then counted who reached the end of the bot flow.[9] 28,000 people viewed the ads. 176 engaged. 10 made it to the final stage, having indicated willingness to commit sabotage. The FSB described this as a "social experiment." They have passed the 10 names to law enforcement "for appropriate action." Whether those people will ever be charged is unclear. The FSB ran the same experiment in November 2025 and publicized it again now — suggesting the goal is deterrence-by-visibility, not just intelligence collection.[9]

The textbooks are the slowest and most durable of the three. Russia's mandatory unified history textbook — written by Putin aide Vladimir Medinsky — will expand to all schools starting in September 2026, beginning in fifth grade.[10] Its executive secretary told Kommersant that students must learn the names and "feats" of soldiers in the "special military operation" to pass the Unified State Exam. On Stalin: "for the first time, a 10th-grade textbook includes his biography and his role in history." On criticism: "only 'foreign agents' criticized" the textbook aggressively. On alternative history: "Why don't we have an alternative multiplication table?"[10]

Key PointThe three operations target different vectors: Scarlet Swan intercepts protest energy before it organizes. The sabotage ads identify and preemptively neutralize potential agents while publicly demonstrating FSB reach. The textbooks shape the cohort that will fight the next war, having never known an alternative account of the current one.[8][9][10]

Afghanistan–Pakistan — Ceasefire Collapsed

Both nuclear-armed states resumed shelling today

A ceasefire between Afghanistan and Pakistan — in effect since March 19 — collapsed today with resumed cross-border shelling in Afghanistan's eastern Kunar Province, killing two people.[1] Afghan forces claim to have destroyed three Pakistani military outposts along the border. Both sides accused each other of breaking the ceasefire first. Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif is simultaneously offering to mediate Iran-US talks while his country's forces are shelling neighboring Afghanistan.[1]

The conflict, ongoing since February 21, involves Pakistan, the Taliban government in Afghanistan, and the Pakistani Taliban (TTP), which operates across the border. Pakistan has conducted airstrikes inside Afghan territory; the Taliban government, which inherited the TTP problem from the previous government, has struck back. No significant international diplomatic effort is underway.

WatchTwo nuclear-armed states exchanging artillery fire along an internationally disputed border, while one of them mediates a separate major conflict — and the world has barely noticed. The Afghanistan-Pakistan conflict has been crowded off the front page by Iran since it started. It belongs on the front page.

Sarah Mullally enthroned as Archbishop of Canterbury: Mullally was enthroned today as the first female primate of the Church of England and Archbishop of Canterbury.[1] The Church of England has existed since 1534. The role has been held by 105 men. The 106th is a woman.

d'Artagnan's bones: Remains believed to be those of Charles de Batz de Castelmore d'Artagnan — the historical musketeer who inspired Dumas — were discovered under a church in Maastricht, Netherlands.[1] D'Artagnan died in 1673 during the Siege of Maastricht. He apparently didn't move far.

Meta and Google liable for child addiction: A Los Angeles jury found Meta and Google liable for a woman's addictions to Instagram and YouTube that began in childhood.[1] This is the first major civil verdict against the platforms on addiction grounds — the legal theory that social media designed to maximize engagement constitutes a defective product. Whether it survives appeal is a different question; that a jury found liability is notable.

Abel Prize — Gerd Faltings: German mathematician Gerd Faltings was awarded the 2026 Abel Prize for his work in arithmetic geometry.[1] Faltings' 1983 proof of the Mordell conjecture was essential groundwork for Andrew Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem a decade later. He's been waiting for this prize a long time.

Science & Discovery

Stone sentinels, 45 new candidates for life, and antimatter on a Swiss road

The Guardians of Rapa Nui beneath the Milky Way — Rositsa Dimitrova

The mo'ai of Ahu Tongariki stand guard over one of Earth's darkest skies. Easter Island's remoteness — thousands of kilometers off South America's coast — gave the Rapa Nui skies dark enough to navigate by, to build a calendar around. The image is a reminder that dark skies are epistemological: the Rapa Nui knew things about the universe that most of the world's population no longer has access to — not because of war or poverty, but because we turned on the lights.

Space — 45 exoplanet candidates for life: Astronomers today published a shortlist of 45 nearby exoplanets as the most promising candidates for habitability, selected based on size, composition, and location within their stars' habitable zones.[11] The catalogue is designed to guide next-generation telescopes searching for biosignatures. At 10.7 light-years, GJ 887 d — announced earlier this month — is the second-nearest confirmed habitable-zone exoplanet after Proxima Centauri b.[11] We are now cataloguing the nearest potentially life-bearing worlds with enough precision to point a telescope at them and look for oxygen.

Physics — Antimatter on the road: Physicists transported antimatter particles by road for the first time yesterday — an estimated 100 to 1,000 antiprotons carried in a Penning trap across 5 kilometers in Switzerland.[11] The goal was to test whether antimatter remains stable during transport, which would enable antimatter-based cancer therapy and fundamental physics experiments at facilities outside CERN. It held together. Antimatter is now, technically, a transportable substance.

Space — Saturn in stereo: NASA's Webb and Hubble telescopes captured a joint image of Saturn in complementary wavelengths, producing the most comprehensive view of the gas giant to date.[12] Webb sees infrared (atmosphere, heat); Hubble sees visible light (rings, storms). Together they reveal a planet that looks fundamentally different depending on which frequencies of light you choose to see.

Environment: A Caltech study published today in Science Advances found that groundwater fluctuations directly influence California seismic activity — higher rates of groundwater level change lead to measurably increased seismic events.[12] This has implications for how drought, atmospheric rivers, and irrigation cycles interact with fault-line stress in the Pacific Northwest corridor.

Key PointToday's publication of 45 exoplanet habitability candidates represents the shift from discovery to shortlisting — from "we found planets" to "here are the ones worth pointing the next telescope at." It's the same transition that happened in human exploration when we went from drawing coastlines to picking specific harbors to investigate.[11]
