2026 Iran War — Conditions, Cluster Bombs, and a Toll Road

Iran rejected the US peace plan but offered five conditions — and is now charging ships to pass through Hormuz

Iran's response to the US 15-point peace plan is being reported as a rejection, but the picture is more complicated.[1] While Press TV initially reported Iran turned it down and set five conditions, a senior Iranian official told Reuters the country is "still reviewing" the proposal.[1] That gap — between an official rejection and ongoing review — is where the real negotiation lives. Pakistan delivered the US plan and offered to host direct talks; that offer remains on the table.[2]

On the ground, the war continued without pause. Iran launched six barrages of missiles at Israel since ISW's last assessment, including cluster munitions striking Bnei Brak that wounded nine people — six children and an elderly woman.[1] Iran also struck a Kuwait International Airport fuel tank with a drone (causing a fire), and Jordan's air force intercepted four missiles and a drone launched from Iran.[1] ISW noted Iran is "likely" using cluster munitions specifically to maximize civilian casualties and terror rather than military effect.[3]

US Central Command reports more than 8,000 targets struck in Iran since the war's start, including 130 Iranian Navy vessels.[1] The IDF has now conducted over 600 strikes targeting Iranian ballistic missile production sites alone, and struck a facility in Esfahan Province that designs and develops submarines and support systems.[3] Despite that, Iran continues to conduct strike operations against multiple countries in the region.

Iran also announced it has intelligence the US and an unnamed "regional country" plan to seize Kharg Island — Iran's primary oil export terminal in the Persian Gulf.[1] The intelligence claim coincides with thousands of US Marines due to arrive in the region in coming days. Kharg Island handles approximately 90% of Iran's crude oil exports; its seizure would be a decisive economic action.

Meanwhile, Iran has formalized its Hormuz toll system. Over 20 vessels have transited via a "Tehran-approved route" through Iranian territorial waters, with Iran requiring payment for passage. Ships linked to the US, Israel, or other "participants in the aggression" are excluded.[3] The Iranian parliament is preparing legislation to codify the fee structure.[1] COSCO Shipping resumed container bookings to Gulf countries after Iran allowed non-hostile vessels through.[1]

Key PointThe Hormuz toll road is a strategic masterstroke Iran is deploying regardless of how peace talks go. By normalizing transit fees, Iran converts the blockade from a blunt weapon into revenue — and establishes a precedent for Iranian sovereignty over the strait that any future agreement will have to navigate. The war's economic resolution may be harder than its military one.[3]
WatchFactCheck documented Trump's claim that Hormuz "doesn't really affect" the US the way it affects other countries as misleading — the US is absorbing global oil price impacts even if direct Gulf imports are modest.[4] But the real story is that the Philippines declared a national energy emergency in response to Iran war fuel risks, Slovenia introduced EU fuel rationing, and QatarEnergy invoked force majeure on LNG contracts to Italy, Belgium, South Korea, and China.[1] The war's global economic blast radius is growing quietly.

Russo-Ukrainian War — The Largest Strike of the War, and a Drone in Estonia

Russia launched nearly 1,000 drones and missiles in one night; one Ukrainian drone hit a power station in EU territory

The night of March 23-24 marked a significant escalation in Russian strike tactics: Russia launched nearly 1,000 drones and missiles in a single prolonged attack — the largest Russian strike series of the entire war.[5] ISW described it as "a significant inflection in Russian strike tactics that allow Russia to threaten more areas of Ukraine for longer periods of time" and said the attacks "disproportionately affect civilian areas."[5]

One strike destroyed the Bernardine Church in Lviv, a UNESCO World Heritage Site in the city's historic center. The church survived centuries of war; it did not survive March 24.[5] Earlier strikes killed three people in Poltava and Zaporizhzhia and damaged residential infrastructure.[5]

Ukraine's response was notable on two fronts. Ukrainian forces destroyed a mobile Zirkon hypersonic missile launcher while it was moving into a firing position in Crimea — ISW flagged this as "a difficult task," requiring precise intelligence on the launcher's position and timing to hit before it fires.[5] A separate Ukrainian overnight strike halted operations at a Russian oil export port.[5]

The spill-over: a stray Ukrainian drone struck a power station in Auvere, Estonia, while another crashed in Latvia during the large-scale drone barrage on Russia.[5] Both are NATO and EU members. No casualties were reported, but the incident underscores how the conflict's operational envelope now reaches into EU territory.

On the Korean Peninsula angle: ISW's March 25 Korea update reports North Korea reinforced its "two hostile states" policy against South Korea during the 15th SPA session on March 22, officially rejecting any prospect of future Pyongyang-Seoul relations.[5] ISW assesses North Korea is actively integrating lessons from the Ukraine war to improve its military equipment and tactics. Trump expressed interest in meeting Kim Jong Un during a planned China visit; ISW assessed the meeting is unlikely given unchanged US North Korea policy.[5]

Key PointNorth Korea absorbing tactical lessons from Ukraine and formally closing the door to South Korea while Lukashenko visits Pyongyang is a quiet strategic alignment that will matter long after the Iran war ends. The autocratic bloc — Russia, North Korea, Belarus — is operating as a coordination unit.[5]

Afghanistan–Pakistan — Ceasefire Collapses, Two Fronts, Both Nuclear

The ceasefire that held for five days ended March 25 with resumed shelling and destroyed Pakistani outposts

The brief ceasefire between Afghanistan and Pakistan — which ran from March 19 to March 24 — has collapsed.[6] On March 25, renewed cross-border shelling killed two people in Afghanistan's eastern Kunar Province. Afghan forces claimed to have destroyed three Pakistani military outposts along the border. Both sides accused the other of breaking the ceasefire first.[6]

The 2026 Afghanistan-Pakistan conflict began February 21 and involves the Afghan Taliban government fighting Pakistan, with the Pakistani Taliban (TTP) as an additional belligerent from Pakistan's perspective. Pakistan's Army, Air Force, and Civil Armed Forces are engaged, while Afghanistan's side includes Hibatullah Akhundzada's Taliban government and the Haqqani network's Sirajuddin Haqqani.[6]

WatchThis is a hot conflict between two nuclear-armed states — India and Pakistan both have nuclear arsenals, and Afghanistan borders Pakistan — that is receiving a fraction of the coverage of the Iran war. The ceasefire collapsed within a week of formation. There is no visible international diplomatic framework managing escalation risk here. Pakistan is simultaneously trying to mediate the Iran war while fighting its own cross-border conflict.[6]

Sudan — Hospital Strike Death Toll Rises to 70

The Al Deain Teaching Hospital attack has now killed 70; RSF captures a town near the Ethiopian border

The death toll from the March 20 drone strike on the Al Deain Teaching Hospital in East Darfur has risen to 70.[7] The attack struck a functioning civilian hospital during active operations. A drone strike on a hospital in an active war zone killing 70 people is a war crime by any standard; it generated roughly none of the coverage that similar attacks in Ukraine or the Middle East receive.

The Rapid Support Forces, alongside Sudan People's Liberation Movement–North rebels, captured the town of Kurmuk in the Blue Nile region near the Ethiopian border.[7] The capture extends RSF and allied control eastward toward Ethiopia, complicating an already-fragile regional picture. A rebel faction led by Yasir Arman separately called on the UN Security Council to end drone use on Sudanese citizens.[7]

The Sudanese civil war, now in its third year, has produced one of the world's worst humanitarian crises — but the news cycle has been swallowed by Iran, Ukraine, and Afghanistan.

Other Consequential Events

Denmark election hung parliament: Danish PM Mette Frederiksen's left-wing bloc won the most seats in the March 24 general election but failed to secure a majority.[7] The election took place in the shadow of the Greenland crisis, which Frederiksen made central to her campaign. A hung result means coalition negotiations, and Denmark's geopolitical posture — NATO's gateway to Greenland — will remain uncertain.

World Baseball Classic: Venezuela defeated the United States in the final of the 2026 World Baseball Classic.[7]

Abel Prize: Mathematician Gerd Faltings was awarded the Abel Prize for his work in arithmetic geometry.[7]

Church of England: Sarah Mullally was enthroned as the first female Primate of the Church of England and Archbishop of Canterbury.[7]

Boko Haram bombings, Maiduguri: Three suicide bombings on March 16 — targeting the University of Maiduguri Teaching Hospital, a post office, and Monday Market — killed 27 and injured 146 in Borno State, Nigeria.[8] Hospital, mail, and market: Boko Haram targeted the full infrastructure of daily civilian life in a single day.

OpenAI shuts down Sora: OpenAI announced it will shut down the Sora app; the concurrent $1 billion Disney-OpenAI deal to license Disney IP for use with Sora was also cancelled.[7]

NASA pauses Lunar Gateway: NASA said it plans to pause the Lunar Gateway orbital station project and redirect resources toward sustained surface operations and a future moonbase.[7]

Meta/Google jury verdict: A Los Angeles jury found Meta and Google liable to a woman who sued over childhood addiction to Instagram and YouTube.[7]

Colombia C-130 crash: At least 66 people were killed when a military Lockheed C-130 carrying 125 people crashed in Puerto Leguízamo, Colombia on March 23.[7]

Australia–EU trade deal: Australia and the EU signed a free trade agreement after eight years of negotiations.[7]

Russia's unified history textbook: Starting September 2026, all Russian schools from fifth grade will use a single standardized history textbook co-authored by Putin aide Vladimir Medinsky.[9] The curriculum includes a section on the "heroes of the special military operation" — students must learn them to pass the college aptitude exam. On Stalin: "For the first time, a 10th-grade textbook includes his biography and his role in history." The project's executive secretary, a presidential administration official, told Kommersant that critics are "foreign agents" and that the textbook has no factual errors.[9]

Russia internet blackout — lived experience: Meduza published reader testimonials from across Russia on life without internet access. Several themes emerged: VPNs still working for now but slowing; economic impact hitting businesses hard ("we switched to email with clients, some installed the Kremlin app Max"); and a consistent assessment that while people are angry, mass protests are not coming. "People swallowed the war and mobilization — they'll swallow this too."[10]

Science & Discovery

This week's science is about things that last: antimatter in a car, 45 worlds worth watching, and ancient sentinels under the Milky Way

The Guardians of Rapa Nui beneath the Milky Way — Rositsa Dimitrova

Space: Astronomers published a shortlist of 45 nearby exoplanets as the most promising candidates for habitability, based on size, composition, and location within their stars' habitable zones.[11] Published March 25, the catalogue is explicitly intended to guide next-generation telescopes searching for biosignatures. This is the field saying: here's where to look first. Separately, Webb and Hubble have teamed up to produce the most comprehensive view of Saturn to date, observing in complementary wavelengths to provide a layered understanding of the gas giant's atmosphere.[12]

Physics: Physicists cut the upper limit on neutrino mass by an order of magnitude using next-generation cryogenic detectors.[12] Neutrinos are among the most common particles in the universe and their mass has direct implications for cosmology — a tenfold improvement in the upper bound is significant. Also from March 24: antimatter particles were transported by road for the first time. An estimated 100–1,000 antiprotons were carried in a Penning trap five kilometers through Switzerland to test their stability during transit.[11] The experiment worked.

Medicine: Scientists from Great Ormond Street Hospital and University College London created the first lab-grown oesophagus that can safely replace a full section of the organ, restore normal swallowing function, and do so without requiring immunosuppression — tested successfully in a growing animal.[11] For children born with esophageal abnormalities, this is a major development in what's surgically possible.

Earth science / Pacific Northwest relevant: A new Caltech study published in Science Advances finds that higher rates of change in groundwater levels lead to measurably increased seismic activity in California — earthquakes vary with the seasons via hydrological pressure on faults.[12] For anyone in the Pacific Northwest where groundwater and snowpack dynamics are substantial: this mechanism operates on the same fault systems.

Key PointThe 45-exoplanet habitability shortlist is a more practical document than it sounds. It's the scientific community's formal prioritization list for a generation of telescopes — Habitable Worlds Observatory and others — that will look for signs of life. The search for life just got a concrete target list.[11]
