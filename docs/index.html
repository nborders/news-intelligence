Secretary of State Marco Rubio is at the G7 foreign ministers' meeting in France today, tasked with shoring up support among allies the Trump administration never consulted before starting the war.[1] Germany's defense minister this week called it out bluntly: Washington has "no exit strategy." EU foreign policy chief Kaja Kallas framed the meeting diplomatically, but the subtext was plain — European governments are watching Trump's war from the outside while absorbing its economic consequences.[1] NATO has not come to the US's aid. Trump warned the alliance this week it would "never forget" the failure, while simultaneously saying the US didn't really need them anyway.[1]

Meanwhile, Iran continues to fight in every dimension available. Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE all reported intercepting missile and drone attacks this morning.[2] The IDF struck ballistic missile production sites and air defense systems across Iran overnight, including sites in Tehran and western Iran.[1] On the diplomatic track, Trump extended the Strait of Hormuz deadline from March 28 to April 6, threatening to destroy Iran's power plants if it isn't reopened — while simultaneously claiming peace talks are "going very well."[1]

The most significant new ISW data: the IRGC has lowered its minimum recruitment age to 12.[3] ISW frames this as a response to "difficulties with recruiting new personnel and managing broader operational disruptions." Combined with CENTCOM's claim that 92% of Iran's largest naval vessels have been destroyed and the launch rate is down 90% from war's start, it tells a coherent story: Iran's conventional military is being systematically degraded, and it is now reaching into its demographic future to replace what it's losing.[3]

The economic cascade is now structural. Southeast Asia — home to 700 million people — imports most of its oil through the Strait of Hormuz. The Philippines declared a national energy emergency this week, with 90% oil import dependency and only about two months of gasoline and diesel on hand.[4] Vietnam Airlines is canceling dozens of domestic flights in April due to jet fuel shortages. Thailand has 61 days of reserves. Singapore has 20–50. Cambodia and Laos, already strained by separate border disruptions, are in the single digits.[4] The Philippines is asking Russia and China for emergency supplies. Vietnam's government is leaning on deep manufacturing relationships with Japan and Korea. Every country in the region is burning through options it doesn't have.

The fertilizer problem, flagged by energy researchers this week, is the hidden third layer: the Persian Gulf states are major fertilizer producers, and output moves through Hormuz. The Strait's effective closure means oil shortages, gas shortages, and food supply shocks heading into planting season. NPR's energy researcher put it plainly: "not a double but a triple whammy."[4]

Zelensky made a surprise visit to Saudi Arabia this week, pitching Ukraine's drone expertise to Gulf states in exchange for air defense missiles. It's a remarkable reframing: Ukraine is now a defense technology exporter to the same regional powers being attacked by Russian-assisted Iranian weapons.[1]

Key PointEurope has declined to join the Iran war. Russia is expanding military cooperation with Iran to facilitate strikes on US and Israeli forces. The US has extended another deadline while peace talks proceed through Pakistan. This is not a war heading toward a clean resolution — it's a war that every party is managing toward different definitions of "over."[1][3]

Russo-Ukrainian War — Ukraine Advances South; Russia Builds the Occupation Road

ISW documents Ukrainian gains and the infrastructure of permanence

ISW's March 26 assessment is more bullish on Ukraine than any in recent weeks.[3] Ukrainian counterattacks continue to advance in southern Ukraine, creating "operational and strategic effects against Russian forces going into the Spring-Summer 2026 offensive against the Fortress Belt." ISW's bottom line: "Russian forces are unlikely to seize Ukraine's Fortress Belt in 2026."[3] Ukrainian forces also recently advanced in Oleksandrivka direction in western Zaporizhia and in northern Kharkiv Oblast, while Russian forces gained ground in the Slovyansk and Pokrovsk directions.[3]

Russia launched 153 drones against Ukraine on March 26. Ukrainian forces responded with long-range strikes on Russian oil infrastructure near the Baltic Sea — ISW notes that drone strikes and Western seizures of Russian tankers are having "significant impacts on Russian oil export revenues," which is the same revenue stream Meduza identified as the Iran war windfall keeping Russia's budget afloat.[3]

Zimbabwe confirmed this week that 15 of its citizens have been killed fighting for Russia in Ukraine, with 60 more still at the front. Information minister Soda Zhemu said most were recruited through social media.[2] It's a data point about Russia's continued difficulty building a sustainable fighting force through legitimate means — and about the transnational reach of whatever recruitment operation is placing Zimbabweans in Russian trenches.

The occupation update from ISW is the counterpoint to the military gains: Russia is planning a road network connecting occupied Ukrainian cities to incentivize investment and physically integrate the territories into Russia permanently. The Nakhimov Naval School is training youth in occupied Ukraine on drone development and operation. Russian military courts sentenced three Ukrainian teenagers to extensive prison terms on likely fabricated terrorism charges.[3]

WatchRussia is simultaneously failing to advance on the military front and succeeding at building permanent occupation infrastructure. The road, the banks, the drone schools — these are designed to make reintegration into Ukraine harder with each passing month. ISW's optimistic front assessment and Russia's infrastructure investment are not contradictory. They're happening on different timescales.[3]

Russia's Information Siege, Continued — "Scarlet Swan," the FSB's Recruitment Ads, and the Delete Campaign

Three stories this week that read like dark comedy, but describe a state actively engineering its own population's behavior

Three Meduza stories this week, read together, sketch an unsettling picture of how Russia is managing the internet crackdown — not just through technical restriction, but through manipulation of information, manufactured crisis, and surveillance theater.

The "delete Telegram" campaign: A coordinated wave of near-identical articles appeared across Russian state-controlled regional media, all urging readers to delete Telegram before March 31, citing cybersecurity risks and (technically baseless) claims that VPNs cause iPhones to overheat.[5] Meduza traced all versions to a single source from February. There is no March 31 deadline. The goal isn't to inform — it's to make the Kremlin's access restrictions look like user-generated concern about safety.

"Scarlet Swan" and the possible FSB trap: A youth protest movement calling itself Scarlet Swan has been organizing on TikTok and Telegram, urging Russians to take to the streets on March 29 against internet restrictions.[6] Opposition journalists and activists have warned that the project may be a security service operation designed to surface dissenters. One of its Telegram administrators' accounts was sold to associates of a pro-war far-right blogger — which the channel then denied in a confusing reversal. The online protest is scheduled for Sunday; the probability that it's a honeypot is being openly discussed.[6]

The FSB's "social experiment": The Sverdlovsk FSB branch ran Telegram ads offering "easy money" — then, when people clicked through, the bot offered options including "carrying out acts of sabotage, disabling infrastructure." The agency later described this as a social experiment to show Russians are susceptible to foreign recruitment. 28,000 people saw the ads; 176 entered the bot; some received offers of 50,000–60,000 rubles for courier work and more for sabotage.[7] Whether it was truly a test or a partially-converted recruitment operation, the FSB published about it on state TV.

Key PointThese three operations — a coordinated misinformation campaign, a possible protest entrapment, and a surveillance theater "experiment" — aren't distinct events. They're the same state capability operating on different targets: the general public (delete your apps), potential dissidents (attend this rally), and susceptible individuals (want quick money?). The Telegram crackdown is not about cutting access. It's about reshaping behavior at scale.[5][6][7]

Denmark Votes, Nepal Swears In, and the IOC Draws a New Line

Three political events that each mark a quiet shift

Denmark held a general election this week, with Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen's Social Democrats remaining the largest party — but no bloc winning a parliamentary majority.[2] Coalition negotiations begin from a hung position. The result is notable for the dog that didn't bark: despite the Iran war's energy pressures and a European security environment that has pushed many governments rightward, the Social Democrats held their ground rather than losing to populist challengers.

Nepal swore in Balendra Shah as Prime Minister today, following the Rastriya Swatantra Party's landslide victory in the March 5 general election.[2] Shah succeeds interim PM Sushila Karki. Separately, lawmakers from the Jammu & Kashmir National Conference protested outside the J&K assembly in solidarity with Iran and against the assassination of former Supreme Leader Khamenei — a reminder that the war's political reverberations are reaching into South Asian regional politics.

The International Olympic Committee announced that starting with the 2028 Los Angeles Olympics, participation in women's events will be limited to athletes classified female based on a one-time genetic test.[2] The policy replaces the previous framework of federation-by-federation rules. The IOC framed it as standardization; the announcement immediately became a political flashpoint in multiple countries.

Other Consequential Events

Iran FIFA World Cup ban: Iran's sports ministry banned the men's national football team from traveling to "hostile countries," putting the team's participation in this summer's World Cup in the US, Canada, and Mexico in serious doubt.[2] Sports minister Donyamali had previously signaled this outcome. The World Cup host country is now also bombing the participating country's military infrastructure.

DHS partial funding deal: The US Senate voted overnight to fund most of the Department of Homeland Security after a 42-day standoff — but the package explicitly excludes ICE and Border Patrol.[8] TSA absences had reached 40% at some airports; more than 480 officers had quit. Democrats framed it as refusing to fund what Schumer called a "rogue and deadly militia without reforms." Republicans indicated the time for negotiating reforms has passed. The House vote remains uncertain; Congress leaves for a two-week recess today.

Belarus–North Korea friendship treaty signed: Lukashenko and Kim Jong Un signed a "friendship and cooperation" treaty in Pyongyang.[2] The institutional alignment of two sanctioned authoritarian states — one supplying artillery to Russia, the other hosting Russian nuclear weapons rhetoric — is being formalized in treaty form.

UK–Russia shadow fleet escalation: Russia vowed a "political, legal and asymmetric" response to the UK's decision to board and detain Russian shadow fleet vessels evading sanctions.[2] British Chief of the General Staff Roland Walker separately warned Parliament that the UK is now on an "inevitable collision course" with Russia and urged the government to prepare.

Portugal Catholic Church abuse compensation: The Catholic Church in Portugal agreed to compensate 57 abuse victims with a total of €1.61 million ($1.86 million), following findings from an independent inquiry into decades of cases.[2]

EU-US trade deal implementation approved: The European Parliament approved legislation implementing the 2025 EU-US trade deal.[2] Quiet institutional progress on transatlantic economic integration, despite political friction elsewhere.

Science & Discovery

The universe's slow violence: galaxies that have been fighting for 100 million years, a Japanese supervolcano quietly refilling, and humans about to orbit the Moon for the first time since 1972

Hickson 44 in Leo — Peter Kennett

Space — Galaxy Group Hickson 44: Today's APOD is a remarkable image of Hickson Compact Group 44, four galaxies locked in gravitational combat about 100 million light-years away in Leo.[9] The edge-on NGC 3190 shows distinctly warped dust lanes from tidal distortion; S-shaped NGC 3187 has been bent by the gravitational tug of its neighbors; the pair are known as Arp 316. All four show enhanced star formation — the gravitational interaction forces gas clouds to collapse and form new stars faster than they otherwise would. These galaxies will eventually merge. The same process is underway in our own Local Group, where the Milky Way and Andromeda are on a multi-billion-year collision course. The image is a snapshot of violence at a scale that makes "ongoing" take on new meaning.[9]

Space — Artemis II Launch Approaching: NASA's Artemis II mission — the first crewed lunar orbit since Apollo 17 in 1972 — is targeting April 2026 for launch.[10] The crew of four will use the Space Launch System heavy-lift rocket and the Orion capsule to travel around the Moon over 10 days, without landing. This is a dress rehearsal for the eventual lunar landing. The launch window is weeks away.

Geology — Japan's Kikai Caldera Is Refilling: A new Kobe University study finds the magma reservoir beneath Kikai caldera in southern Japan has been steadily refilling since its cataclysmic eruption 7,300 years ago — the largest volcanic eruption of the Holocene epoch.[11] The findings help researchers build general models for supervolcano behavior, including Yellowstone and the Toba caldera in Indonesia. The key insight: the refilling rate suggests monitoring can detect precursor signals decades before any future eruption becomes imminent. No immediate risk — but the caldera is now understood as active rather than dormant.

Physics — Hunting Axions with Piezoelectrics: A new approach to detecting axions — the hypothetical dark matter particles that would be extremely light and weakly interacting — uses piezoelectric materials to convert axion-induced mechanical vibrations into measurable electrical signals.[12] Most dark matter detection methods rely on axions interacting with electromagnetic fields; this mechanical approach opens a new detection window. Dark matter is estimated to make up roughly 85% of all matter in the universe; we have yet to directly detect a single particle of it.

Key PointTwo things are converging this spring: humanity's first crewed lunar orbit in 54 years, and a growing arsenal of new techniques to probe the fundamental composition of the universe. The Artemis II crew will look back at Earth from cislunar space as physicists close in on what most of the cosmos is actually made of.[9][10]

Dispatch from the Absurd

The IRGC's solution to its manpower crisis

ISW reported this week, without editorial comment, that the IRGC has lowered its minimum recruitment age to 12.[3]

The context: 92% of Iran's largest naval vessels have reportedly been destroyed. Its launch rate is down 90% from war's start. Its Navy Commander was killed in Bandar Abbas three days ago. The IRGC is "facing difficulties with recruiting new personnel."

So: twelve.

Dispatch from the AbsurdThe IRGC's answer to a devastating air campaign against its military-age population is to expand the definition of "military age" downward until it touches middle school. Meanwhile, in the parallel information war, Iran is reportedly firing memes at Trump on social media. One front: missiles and drones with a 90% degraded launch rate. The other: the internet. The memes are probably more effective.[3][1]
