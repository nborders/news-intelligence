2026 Iran War — The Toll Booth at the Edge of the World

A war entering its second month now includes a fee to use the world's most strategic waterway

The Iran war crossed the 25-day mark with no end in sight and a new development that would be absurd if it weren't consequential: Iran is now charging ships to transit the Strait of Hormuz. ISW-CTP reported overnight that more than 20 vessels have taken a "Tehran-approved route" through Iranian territorial waters — effectively a tolled lane through the blockade.[1] The Iranian parliament is reportedly preparing legislation to formalize fees for "safe passage."[2] Iran also said non-hostile vessels can pass if they comply with its "safety and security requirements," while explicitly excluding vessels from countries it says are involved in the war.[2]

The diplomatic picture remained fractured. Trump extended his ultimatum again — the new deadline for Iran to deal is March 27.[3] Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf publicly called reports of US-Iran negotiations "fake news intended to manipulate financial and oil markets."[3] But Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson simultaneously acknowledged responding "through intermediaries of friendly countries" to requests.[4] Pakistan, Egypt, Oman, and Turkey are all running backchannels. The US sent Iran a 15-point plan to end the war, according to the New York Times.[2]

The ground picture as of March 25 per ISW: Iran has launched nine waves of missiles targeting Israel since ISW's previous data cutoff. Iran continued firing cluster munitions on March 24, which ISW notes are "likely to maximize destruction and terrorize Israeli civilians."[1] Hezbollah claimed 54 attacks on Israeli forces and positions in a 24-hour window.[1] The IDF has now struck over 3,000 targets in Iran — over 600 of them ballistic missile sites — and announced "extensive" strikes targeting production facilities in Esfahan Province on March 24.[1] Israel's Defense Minister Katz stated the IDF will control a "security zone" up to the Litani River in southern Lebanon until "the threat of Hezbollah" is removed — and suggested Israel would stay after the conflict ends.[2]

Lebanon expelled Iran's ambassador, giving him until March 29 to leave — the government trying to separate its fate from Tehran's choices in a war Hezbollah dragged it into.[2] Gold prices in Dubai, which had dropped 15 days running on diplomacy signals, rose again overnight.[2]

Key PointIran's Hormuz toll system is strategically revealing. It shows Iran has modulated from "close the strait completely" to "control who passes and charge for it." That's a negotiating position dressed as military posture — it preserves leverage while creating a de-facto revenue stream and giving participating vessels an off-ramp. The question is whether the US and Israel treat the toll lanes as evidence Iran is ready to deal, or as evidence Iran is fortifying a long-term control position.
WatchTrump's stated deadline is now March 27 — but Israel's military leadership has said it needs several more weeks to achieve its war objectives.[4] These clocks are not synchronized. When Trump declares victory on March 27, the IDF will likely still be targeting Iranian ballistic missile infrastructure. That gap between Washington's political timeline and Tel Aviv's military one is the most structurally dangerous feature of the current phase.

Russo-Ukrainian War — The Largest Strike of the War

Russia saturates Ukrainian airspace in a single overnight barrage

Russia launched nearly 1,000 drones and missiles against Ukraine over the nights of March 23-24 — the single largest strike series in the entire war.[5] ISW describes it as "a significant inflection in Russian strike tactics that allow Russia to threaten more areas of Ukraine for longer periods of time and disproportionately affect civilian areas."[5] Russian forces killed at least three people in the barrage, including two in Poltava and one in Zaporizhzhia, while damaging residential buildings and critical infrastructure across multiple oblasts.[6]

One of the strikes destroyed the Bernardine Church in Lviv's Old Town, a UNESCO World Heritage Site.[5] The church had survived centuries intact; it did not survive the night of March 23.

Ukraine delivered its own technical achievement during the barrage. Ukrainian forces destroyed a ground-based Zirkon hypersonic missile launcher while it was moving into a firing position in Crimea on the night of March 23-24.[5] ISW noted this as "a difficult task" — detecting and hitting a mobile Zirkon launcher before it fires is a significant intelligence and targeting achievement. Separately, a Ukrainian overnight strike halted operations at a Russian oil port used for exports.[5]

Russia recently launched satellites designed to serve as a domestic Starlink analog for military use.[5] If successful, this would reduce Russian dependence on civilian Telegram channels for battlefield coordination — the same vulnerability Meduza's reporting has been tracking as the Kremlin blocks civilian access to the app while exempting frontline troops.

On the ground, Ukrainian forces continue to advance in the Slovyansk direction and in the Kostyantynivka-Druzhkivka tactical area, and have made gains in western Zaporizhzhia Oblast.[5] This continues the pattern Meduza's March 19 battlefield analysis identified: Ukraine trading defensive exposure north of Pokrovsk for offensive pressure in the south.

Key PointThe 1,000-drone strike deserves its own standing in the historical record of this war. Russia has been steadily increasing strike density, but this jump in magnitude — a single overnight package bigger than anything before it — signals that Russia has stockpiled and is now deploying mass-saturation tactics. The goal isn't just damage; it's overwhelming air defenses, exhausting interceptor supplies, and extending the threat window across a larger geographic area simultaneously. This is a new operational mode, not just a larger version of the old one.[5]

Sudan — Two Active Catastrophes

The death toll from a hospital drone strike reached 70; the RSF is expanding eastward

The March 20 drone strike on the Al Deain Teaching Hospital in East Darfur has now killed at least 70 people — up from the initial count of around 30.[6] A coalition of Rapid Support Forces and Sudan People's Liberation Movement–North (SPLM-N) also captured Kurmuk in the Blue Nile region, near the Ethiopian border.[6] A Sudanese rebel faction led by Yasir Arman called on the UN Security Council to end drone strikes on Sudanese civilians.[6]

The RSF's capture of Kurmuk extends its control toward the Ethiopian border, in a conflict that has killed more people and displaced more civilians than any other crisis in the world but receives a fraction of the media attention of the Iran war. The hospital strike — targeting functioning medical infrastructure during active fighting — has drawn no significant international response.

WatchSudan has produced the world's largest displacement crisis — more than 12 million people driven from their homes — while international attention has been consumed almost entirely by Iran, Ukraine, and US domestic politics. The RSF's expansion into the Blue Nile region threatens to pull the conflict deeper into territory that borders Ethiopia, where tensions are already high. A hospital strike that kills 70 people would dominate global headlines if it happened in a more-covered conflict. In Sudan, it barely registers.

Other Consequential Events

Denmark hung parliament: Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen's left-wing bloc won the most seats in Tuesday's general election but failed to secure a majority — a result that leaves her seeking coalition partners after a campaign conducted in the shadow of the Greenland sovereignty crisis and Trump's territorial ambitions.[7] Frederiksen had staked her third-term campaign partly on her handling of the US relationship.

Abel Prize — Gerd Faltings: German mathematician Gerd Faltings was awarded the 2026 Abel Prize — mathematics' equivalent of the Nobel — for his work in arithmetic geometry.[7] Faltings' theorem (proving the Mordell conjecture in 1983) established that algebraic curves of genus ≥ 2 over number fields have only finitely many rational points. It was essential groundwork for Andrew Wiles' 1995 proof of Fermat's Last Theorem. The field is abstract; the lineage is extraordinary.

OpenAI shuts down Sora: OpenAI announced it will shut down its Sora video-generation app, simultaneously cancelling a $1 billion investment deal that had allowed Disney's intellectual properties to be used with the tool.[7] The AI boom/bust cycle, compressed.

Epic Games layoffs: Epic Games announced 1,000+ layoffs alongside $500M in cuts to contracting and marketing, citing a sharp decline in the number of players on Fortnite.[7]

Australia–EU free trade agreement: Australia and the European Union signed a free trade deal after eight years of negotiations.[7]

Robert Mueller, 81: The former FBI director and special counsel died this week, per Wikipedia's recent deaths.[6] His investigation defined Washington for two years and produced 448 pages that Congress declined to act on. The news cycle moved on quickly.

Science & Discovery

The Rapa Nui sentinels watch the sky while the species they guard disappears below

The Guardians of Rapa Nui beneath the Milky Way — Rositsa Dimitrova

The mo'ai of Ahu Tongariki stand guard over one of Earth's darkest skies. The Rapa Nui (Easter Island) figures face inward — their backs to the Pacific, their faces toward the Milky Way, which arcs overhead in this image captured by astrophotographer Rositsa Dimitrova. The island's remoteness, thousands of kilometers off South America's coast, gave the Rapa Nui something rare: skies dark enough to navigate by, to calibrate calendars by, to build a civilization around. The image is a reminder that dark skies aren't just aesthetic — they're epistemological. The Rapa Nui knew things about the universe that most of the world's population no longer has access to, not because of war or poverty, but because we turned on the lights.

Space — AI exoplanet hunt: Astronomers at the University of Warwick used a new AI tool on data from NASA's TESS satellite to validate over 100 exoplanets, including 31 previously undetected worlds.[8] The tool identifies the subtle light-dimming patterns of planets transiting in front of stars — a signal that human reviewers often miss in large datasets. At this pace, the catalog of confirmed exoplanets will grow faster than our ability to characterize them.

Ecology — Fish migration collapse: A study published in Nature warns that the world's great freshwater fish migrations are collapsing. These movements — which rival the Serengeti wildebeest migration in sheer biomass — are driven by species that spawn in rivers and feed in seas, or vice versa, and which underpin food security for hundreds of millions of people worldwide.[9] Dams, habitat loss, and climate change are converging to eliminate migrations that took millions of years to evolve.

Previously noted, still developing: The antimatter transport experiment (March 24), the lab-grown esophagus implant (March 20), and CERN's doubly charmed baryon confirmation (March 17) all cleared their initial benchmarks last week and remain among the most significant science events of the month.

Key PointThe fish migration story and the exoplanet count landing in the same week creates an uncomfortable juxtaposition: we're adding planets to the catalog faster than we're keeping species alive on the one we already have. Both are legitimate science. The second one has a policy window that's closing.
