2026 Iran War — The One-Month Mark

A conflict where everyone is winning and nobody will stop

The Iran war is approaching its one-month anniversary with the strange quality of a war that has produced more narratives of victory than actual resolution. President Trump declared on Tuesday that "we've won this. This war has been won" — while simultaneously extending his five-day deadline on striking Iran's power grid and sending thousands more Marines to the Middle East.[1] Iran's Foreign Ministry denied direct negotiations are even happening.[2] And two Israeli military officials told NPR their forces need several more weeks of fighting to complete their war aims, saying they are "in many ways halfway there."[3]

ISW's ground picture is more specific than the rhetoric. The IDF has struck over 3,000 targets across Iran since the war began, including over 50 sites overnight March 23-24.[4] Iran continues to launch ballistic missiles with cluster-munition warheads — one cluster bomb impacted a Haifa suburb, another ballistic missile hit Tel Aviv, damaging apartment buildings and injuring four people, evading US and Israeli defense systems.[4] Iran's SNSC has a new secretary: Mohammad Bagher Zolghadr, a former IRGC deputy commander, replacing Ali Larijani whom the IDF killed on March 17.[4] Leadership decapitation is real; the war is not slowing.

Lebanon expelled Iran's ambassador — a significant diplomatic rupture signaling Beirut's government trying to separate itself from the conflict killing its citizens.[5] Hezbollah claimed 55 attacks on Israeli positions in a single 24-hour window.[4] A projectile hit Iran's Bushehr nuclear power plant.[1] The Philippines declared a national energy emergency linked to Iran war fuel disruptions; oil briefly hit $100/barrel before dropping 11% on Trump's talk of negotiations.[6]

Pakistan, Egypt, Oman, and Turkey are all running backchannels. Pakistan's prime minister publicly offered to host talks. An Egyptian official confirmed the intermediary network is active.[3] The strategic question is whether Trump's "five days" ultimatum is a genuine diplomatic opening or a pressure tactic — and whether Iran's denial of talks is a face-saving posture or a real refusal.

Key PointThere are two diverging clocks in this war. Trump's political clock says "it's won, let's deal." Israel's military clock says "we need more weeks." These aren't coordinated. That gap — between a US president who wants the win and an Israeli military that hasn't achieved its objectives — is where the next phase of this conflict will be decided.[3]
WatchFactCheck has documented a cluster of unsupported or misleading claims around the Iran war: Trump claimed the Strait of Hormuz closure "doesn't really affect" the US (false — global oil prices affect the US directly)[7]; Trump's claim that a bombed Iranian school building "was done by Iran" contradicts video, satellite, and expert evidence pointing to a US strike[8]; arms control experts dispute Trump's framing of Iran's nuclear and missile capabilities as his stated casus belli.[9] When the factual scaffolding of a war keeps failing fact-checks, the stated rationale and the actual rationale have diverged.

Russo-Ukrainian War — Spring Trade-Offs

Ukraine gains ground in the south by spending it in the north

Meduza's March 19 battlefield analysis shows Ukrainian forces executing a calculated operational gamble: concentrating airborne brigades and assault regiments to strike at the junction between Russia's 29th and 36th armies in the Zaporizhzhia-Dnipropetrovsk region, pushing 10-15 kilometers into Russian rear positions.[10] The aim is to encircle the 36th Army — or at least cut its supply routes. Russian command has responded by redeploying reserves, including naval infantry brigades pulled from near Pokrovsk.

The cost: Ukraine redeployed defenders from the Pokrovsk sector to enable the southern strikes, opening space for a new Russian offensive toward Dobropillia. ISW confirmed Ukrainian advances in the Slovyansk direction as of March 24, alongside continued Russian pressure across multiple axes.[11] A Russian Shahed drone struck and severely damaged the Bernardine Church in Lviv — a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Russian strikes killed three civilians across Ukraine on March 24.[12] US and Ukrainian delegations met again in Miami on March 22; Russia launched 251 drones in a single night.[11]

Key PointUkraine's spring counteroffensive is ISW-verified and real, but it's a forced trade: gains in Zaporizhzhia are purchased by degrading defenses north of Pokrovsk. Ukraine doesn't have the force depth to do both simultaneously. The counteroffensive buys leverage; it doesn't resolve the fundamental disparity in manpower.[10]

Russia's Information Siege

Telegram, textbooks, and a blogger who checked himself in

Three Meduza stories this week form a coherent picture of how Russia is managing its information environment as the war and economic pressure intensify.

The internet crackdown. Meduza surveyed Russian readers about life under mobile internet throttling and Telegram blocking — which the Kremlin appears to have executed two weeks ahead of its stated April timeline.[13] The responses are striking not for rage but for adaptation. "The era of free and accessible internet is over," writes a reader from Omsk. "I don't think there will be mass protests — everyone around me has just adapted." A reader from Moscow notes that in the metro, "people are still texting and scrolling like before" because whitelists cover "almost all their needs."[14] The consensus: VPNs still work for those who care. Most people don't care enough to protest over it.

The textbook. Starting September 2026, all Russian schools from grade 5 onward will use a single state-authored history textbook co-created by Putin aide Vladimir Medinsky. Stalin gets a biography "for the first time," because excluding him from WWII accounts was wrong. Gorbachev gets no blame for Soviet collapse — students are invited to "draw their own conclusions." Prince Vladimir's fratricide is omitted because sixth graders might remember it instead of the Christianization of Rus. And students must "know the heroes of the special military operation" to pass university entrance exams.[15] It is a syllabus for citizenship.

The blogger. Ilya Remeslo — a longtime pro-Kremlin operator paid to target Navalny — published a Telegram post on March 17 listing five reasons he stopped supporting Putin, calling for Putin to resign and stand trial as a war criminal. Days later, he was hospitalized at a St. Petersburg psychiatric facility that historically housed Soviet-era dissidents.[16] Whether this is punitive psychiatry, a self-protective maneuver (checking in to avoid prosecution), or a genuine breakdown remains unclear. The facility's history makes the optics chilling regardless of the actual cause.

WatchThe "Narva People's Republic" — anonymous accounts on Telegram, TikTok, and VK calling for Estonian Ida-Viru County to secede — follows the exact template Russia used before the Donbas "people's republics" in 2014.[17] Estonia's intelligence calls it a "simple and low-cost" information attack. The channel had 60-70 followers when first flagged; it exceeded 800 after media coverage. The 1993 Narva autonomy referendum (97% support, declared illegal by Estonian courts) is real history that exists as substrate for this campaign. The debate about whether to cover it — amplifying a fringe view vs. leaving it to normalize without context — is genuinely unresolved and being actively debated inside Estonia right now.

Other Consequential Events

Danish general election: Danes, Faroese, and Greenlanders voted today. Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen seeks a third term against the backdrop of the Greenland sovereignty crisis — Trump's territorial ambitions having become a live election issue in the country that administers the territory he wants.[18]

Congo presidential election: Denis Sassou Nguesso, who has ruled the Republic of the Congo intermittently since 1979, was proclaimed the winner.[19] No surprises.

Colombia military crash: At least 66 people killed when a Colombian Air Force C-130 carrying 125 people crashed in Puerto Leguízamo on March 23.[20]

Kenya flash floods: At least 84 people killed across Kisumu, Uasin Gishu, and Tana River counties.[21]

Maiduguri bombings: 26 killed, 146 injured in suspected Boko Haram bombings in Nigeria's Borno State — a city that has been at the center of the insurgency for over a decade.[22]

World Baseball Classic: Venezuela defeated the United States in the final; Maikel García named tournament MVP.[23]

Africa Cup of Nations reversal: CAF overturned the 2025 AFCON final result — originally won by Senegal — declaring Morocco the winner.[24]

NASA moonbase pivot: NASA announced it will pause the Lunar Gateway project and redirect toward sustained moonbase infrastructure on the lunar surface, with Artemis II (the first crewed lunar orbit in decades) still scheduled for 2026.[25]

Science & Discovery

The week the universe got stranger in four directions at once

Today's APOD is a video: a gravity map of Earth built from 15 years of data from NASA's twin GRACE satellites, showing in color where the gravitational field is stronger or weaker. A low-gravity trough sits off India's coast; a relative high crowns the mountains of Chile. The causes often trace not to surface features but to deep mantle structures — the planet's hidden interior, still reshaping things from above. Worth watching: NASA APOD — A Gravity Map of Earth A Gravity Map of Earth — NASA

Physics — Antimatter transport: Antiprotons were transported by road for the first time in history today — an estimated 100-1,000 antiprotons carried in a Penning trap across five kilometers in Switzerland.[26] The experiment tests antimatter stability during transit, a step toward portable antimatter sources for medical imaging and fundamental physics. The timeline is notable: this happened on the same day Iran was firing ballistic missiles at Israeli cities.

Medicine — Lab-grown esophagus: Scientists at Great Ormond Street Hospital and UCL successfully implanted a fully lab-grown esophagus in a growing animal, restoring normal swallowing without immunosuppression.[27] This is a proof-of-concept for children born with absent or severely malformed food pipes — a condition requiring complex, often unsuccessful surgery. The organ grew with the animal rather than being rejected.

Particle physics — CERN: The LHCb experiment confirmed the doubly charmed baryon Ξcc⁺ — a particle containing two charm quarks and one down quark — resolving a 20-year discrepancy in experimental results.[28] Closes a gap in the Standard Model's particle catalog.

Climate: The World Meteorological Organization's State of the Global Climate report found Earth's energy imbalance and ocean heat content hit record levels in 2025.[29] A separate March 9 study in Geophysical Research Letters confirmed with statistical confidence that warming has accelerated since 2015, rising from 0.2°C to 0.35°C per decade — suggesting the 1.5°C threshold arrives before 2030 if trends hold.[30]

Key PointThe antimatter transport and the lab-grown esophagus arrived in the same week. One is pure physics curiosity with decades-off applications; the other is a direct intervention in children's suffering. Both required decades of quiet, unglamorous precision work to get here. The week's dominant news is all about what humans destroy. These are what humans build.

Dispatch from the Absurd

Dispatch from the AbsurdPro-Kremlin blogger Ilya Remeslo spent years filing lawsuits against Navalny, testified for the prosecution at trial, and collected Kremlin money to write pro-war content. On March 17 he posted a screed calling Putin a war criminal and asking for donations. Days later he was hospitalized in a psychiatric facility that once housed Soviet dissidents. He gave a calm TV interview beforehand explaining his reasoning: Putin has a "bad horoscope" and the regime "doesn't have long left." The Akhmat special forces commander called and asked him to delete the post. He refused, quoted a rock musician at him, and hung up. Then he checked himself in. Whether that last move was self-protection, genuine crisis, or an attempt to seem untouchable is unanswerable. What is answerable: a man who spent a decade building the cage discovered the door was locked from the outside — and a Soviet-era psychiatric hospital was his best option for a safe room.[16]
